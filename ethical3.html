Weekly excess deaths from natural causes in ages 0-4

Ethical Skeptic posted this plot of weekly excess deaths in ages 0-4, where he got about 77% excess deaths on weeks 14 to 20 of 2025: [https://web.archive.org/web/20250903223011/https://theethicalskeptic.com/2025/08/19/houston-we-have-another-problem/]

Despite numerous requests, ES has refused to document how he calculated his baseline, and he hasn't even posted any plot that would have one line for the actual deaths and another line for the baseline, but he has only posted a plot which shows the excess deaths, where it's not even transparent if the excess deaths are going up because the actual deaths are going up, or because the baseline is going down.

Therefore in order to reverse engineer his baseline, I digitized the excess deaths in his plot, and I subtracted them from the actual weekly deaths that I downloaded from CDC WONDER. ChatGPT and Grok were not successful in digitizing the weekly number of excess deaths, so I used a tool called WebPlotDigitizer instead:

  1. Upload the plot here: https://automeris.io/wpd/.
  2. Click on the first week of the blue line to set the X1 coordinate, click on the last week of the blue line to set the X2 coordinate, and then click the y-axis at points for -50 and 210. In the sidebar set Y1 to -50 and Y2 to 210, and click "Skip rotation correction".
  3. Under "Mask", select the "Pen" tool and highlight the area of the blue line, and select the "Erase" tool and erase the area of the other lines.
  4. Select the color of the blue line from the color picker in the sidebar.
  5. Change "Algorithm" to "X Step w/ Interpolation", set "ΔX Step" to 0.00265252, and click "Run" (where the value of the X step is 1 divided by 377, and 377 comes from how there are 378 weeks from week 8 of 2018 up to week 20 of 2025, which is the approximate range of weeks shown in the plot). Check the bottom of the left sidebar if you got 378 data points, and if not, change the "ΔX Step" parameter until you get exactly 378 data points.
  6. Click "View Data" and copy the y-axis values from the output.

What ICD codes are included in the plot?

ES posted this screenshot that showed what ICD codes were included in his plot: [https://theethicalskeptic.com/2025/08/19/houston-we-have-another-problem/]

The U chapter consists of ICD codes for terrorism (U01-03), SARS (U04), vaping-related disorder (U07.0), COVID (U07.1), and long COVID (U09). The code for long COVID is only used as a multiple cause of death and not as an underlying cause. Apart from U07.1 deaths, ages 0-4 had only 4 deaths in 1999-2025 with the underlying cause under the U chapter, which consist of 3 deaths in September 2001 with the UCD U01.1 (terrorism involving destruction of aircraft), and one death in 2022 with the UCD U04.9 (SARS) which is probably a miscoded COVID death, so it doesn't make much difference if you exclude the entire U chapter or only U07.1.

ES seems to have also included R codes in his plot, even though you can't see from his screenshot if he included R codes or not. Out of deaths in ages 0-4 in 2018-2025 with an R code as the underlying cause, about 97% of all deaths had the underlying cause R99 ("Other ill-defined and unspecified causes of mortality") or R95 ("Sudden infant death syndrome - SIDS"):

R99 and R96 deaths are elevated in recent months, because R99 and R96 are used as temporary placeholder codes for deaths that will later be assigned under a specific cause. However R95 (SIDS) does not appear to be used as a similar placeholder code, and SIDS deaths are not elevated in recent months like R99 and R96 deaths.

CDC WONDER's documentation says that deaths from external causes and SIDS are listed under the 999--999 placehold category for 24 weeks prior to the final date in the provisional data (which in turn is about 4 weeks before the current week): "In the provisional mortality data, causes of death classified as external causes of injury (ICD-10 codes V01-Y89), sudden deaths (ICD-10 codes R95 and R96), or drug poisoning (ICD-10 codes T36-T50), have the cause of death labeled as 'Data not shown due to 6 month lag to account for delays in death certificate completion for certain causes of death.' For these deaths occurring 24 weeks prior to the final date in the provisional data, the number of deaths and related statistics are reported as Not Available." [https://wonder.cdc.gov/wonder/help/mcd-provisional.html]

When I did a query at CDC WONDER on MMWR week 38 of 2025, the final date included in the provisional data was on week 34, so deaths from external causes and SIDS were only returned up to week 10, and 999--999 deaths were returned from week 11 up to week 34.

Deaths from external causes are listed under 999--999 for the last 24 weeks of the provisional data, so in fact Ethical Skeptic's plot includes deaths from external causes for several weeks at the end of his x-axis. An earlier version of his plot extended up to week 15 of 2025, but on this page I have written about a version of his plot that extends up to week 20 of 2025, which he first posted around MMWR week 34 of 2025. [https://x.com/EthicalSkeptic/status/1957560098366042437/photo/1] But if on week 34 the last week included in the provisional data was week 30, then deaths from external causes would've been listed under 999--999 from week 7 onwards (which would've been the last 14 weeks displayed in Ethical Skeptic's plot).

2010-2019 linear trend for deaths and ASMR with 95% PI

Ethical Skeptic got 25 sigma excess deaths on weeks 14 to 20 of 2025 in his plot. The high sigma score can be explained by his baseline being too steep, combined with deaths in 2025 being inflated by 999--999 and R99 deaths, and his short 2-year baseline producing inflated z-scores.

ES calculates the sigma score by subtracting the baseline deaths from the actual deaths, and then dividing the result by the standard deviation of the residual deaths in the baseline fitting period (i.e. actual deaths minus baseline deaths in 2018-2019). If you fit a seasonality-adjusted baseline against only 2 years of data, you tend to get a low residual number of deaths in the fitting period, because the baseline ends up adapting to whatever the deaths happen to be during the baseline period, so therefore you get a low standard deviation of the residuals, and you subsequently get high sigma scores. If you use his method to fit a seasonality-adjusted baseline against only one year of data, you can get a standard deviation of zero for the residuals during the fitting period, and therefore you subsequently get infinite sigma scores. But similarly a 2-year baseline tends to produce lower residuals than a longer baseline.

In order to estimate how the number of deaths compares to the past range of variation, a better method is to calculate a 95% prediction interval for the past trend.

Failed attempt to reproduce exponential baseline

At some point ES edited his blog post to add a link to this image, which is intended to explain how he calculated his baseline: [https://theethicalskeptic.com/2025/08/19/houston-we-have-another-problem/]

He seems to have fitted his baseline against data starting from 2018 and not 2014, he seems to have excluded certain weeks from his baseline fitting period, he may have taken a moving average of the deaths before or after he calculated the baseline, and he may have added an additional step to calculate the baseline for week 53, but I don't know if there's other ways how his methodology was different from the image above.

I attempted to replicate his methodology in the following code:

# get weekly deaths in ages 0-4 with the underlying cause A-R or 999--999
t=fread("https://sars2.net/f/wondervaccinial0to4.csv")

# convert deaths to a 6-week moving average
# the window extends up to 3 weeks backwards and up to 2 weeks forwards
# the window extends 0 weeks backwards and 2 weeks forwards on the first week
# ES may have excluded the first 3 weeks instead of using a narrower backwards window like me
ma=\(x,b=1,f=b)rowMeans(embed(c(rep(NA,b),x,rep(NA,f)),f+b+1),na.rm=T)
t[,dead:=ma(dead,3,2)]

# calculate the baseline using weeks of the MMWR years 2018 and 2019
# I excluded weeks 51 and 52 of 2019, but I don't know what weeks were actually excluded by ES
base=t[year<2020&!(year==2019&week>50)]

# get average deaths for each week number during the baseline period
# I treated week 53 as an average of weeks 52 and 1, but I don't know how ES treated week 53
weekly=base[,tapply(dead,week,mean)]
weekly[53]=mean(weekly[c(1,52)])

# get change in weekly average number of deaths between 2018 and 2019 (about -12.3)
# this is similar to the SLOPE function in Excel which was probably used by ES
slope=coef(lm(dead~year,base[year<2020]))[2]

# get annual growth rate relative to average weekly deaths in 2018-2019 (about -0.0295 or -2.95%)
growthrate=slope/base[year<2020,mean(dead)]

# get final baseline
t[,baseline:=weekly[week]*(1+growthrate)^(year-2018.5)]

If you do a linear regression of weekly deaths in 2018-2019 against a year variable, where there's 52 weeks where the value of the year variable is 2018 and 52 weeks where the value of the year variable is 2019, it's equivalent to doing a linear regression of yearly averages of weekly deaths, and the second coefficient produced by both methods is the same as the average deaths in 2018 subtracted from the average deaths in 2019:
