Short version

Fenton wrote that "Between 2016 and 2022, I attended many research seminars in the UK on using AI and Machine Learning to 'combat misinformation'." His coauthor Scott McLachlan replied that "you'll recall I attended several too" and that one of the seminars was organized by David Spiegelhalter.

Spiegelhalter later wrote a book about COVID statistics. Nudge expert Magda Osman

Fenton has coauthored many papers with the psychologist Magda Osman, who used to be a faculty member at the Queen Mary University of London. [https://scholar.google.com/scholar?q=norman+fenton+magda+osman] In June 2021, McLachlan, Osman, Fenton, and three other people from Queen Mary University published a preprint about vaccine deaths at VAERS. [https://www.researchgate.net/publication/352837543_Analysis_of_COVID-19_vaccine_death_reports_from_the_Vaccine_Adverse_Events_Reporting_System_VAERS_Database_Interim_Results_and_Analysis]

Magda Osman is a researcher of misinformation, which is interesting considering how Fenton and McLachlan attended several seminars about combating misinformation. In 2022 Magda Osman was the first author of a paper published by Journal of Risk Research titled "People's understanding of the concept of misinformation", and in 2024 Osman published a paper titled "Public Evaluations of Misinformation and Motives for Sharing It". [https://bmeder.org/publication/2022-03-18_osman_et_al-2022-misinformation/2022-03-18_Osman_et_al-2022-misinformation.pdf, https://www.mdpi.com/2673-5172/5/2/50]

In 2020, Osman, McLachlan, Fenton, Neil, and two other authors published a paper about nudging as a strategy of behavior modification. Queen Mary University of London published an article about the paper, where they quoted Magda Osman as saying: "We believe that causal analysis can advance existing behaviour change frameworks as they allow us to formally model behaviour change problems and the context in which these interventions are situated. By incorporating these approaches into the early design of behavioural interventions, we can begin to understand what factors are relevant to the success of the intervention and how the intervention could influence these factors, and even prepare precautionary measures to help avoid failure." [https://www.qmul.ac.uk/sbbs/news/items/nudges-fail-more-often-than-is-reported-experts-warn.html] So even though she presented herself as a critic of the nudge method, the reason why she appears to have been critical of the method was because it sometimes failed as a method of behavior modification, and not because she thought that the government had no business engaging in covert schemes of behavior modification.

In 2018 Fenton and Neil coauthored another paper with Magda Osman about nudging as a technique of behavior modification. The abstract of the paper said: "Social policy interventions, such as nudges (behavioral change techniques), have gained significant traction globally. But what do the public think? Does the type of expert proposing a nudge influence the kinds of evaluations the public make about nudges? Three experiments investigated this by presenting U.S. (N = 689) and U.K. (N = 978) samples with descriptions of nudges (genuine and fictitious) proposed by either scientists or the government. Overall, compared to opaque and fictitious nudges, transparent and genuine nudges were judged more ethical and plausible, and scientists proposing them were judged more trustworthy than a government working group. Also, trust in fictitious interventions proposed by scientists was higher than in genuine interventions proposed by a government working group." [https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/abs/10.1080/01973533.2018.1469986] Queen Mary University of London published an article about the paper, where they quoted Fenton as saying "While people judged genuine nudges as more plausible than fictitious nudges, people trusted some fictitious nudges proposed by scientists as more plausible than genuine nudges proposed by government" (which sounds just like alt media, where people readily believe outlandish claims as long as the source of the claim is a former chief scientist at Pfizer, or the inventor of the PCR test, or great Bayesian eggheads from London). [https://www.qmul.ac.uk/media/news/2018/se/people-trust-scientific-experts-more-than-the-government-even-when-the-evidence-is-outlandish-.html]
