Origin of the visna recombination theory

A variant of Nick's theory that HIV is a recombinant of visna and some other virus had earlier been presented by his mentor Alan Cantwell. Cantwell in turn appears to have copied the theory from the Strecker brothers, who had presented it by March 1986 or earlier. Other variants of the visna recombination theory had been presented by John Seale by December 1985 or earlier, and by Jakob Segal by May 1986 or earlier. However I'm not sure if the Strecker brothers, Segal, or Seale came up with the theory first.

John Seale initially suggested that HIV was manufactured by adding an extra gene to the visna virus without specifying where the extra gene came from, but later he said that HIV was a recombinant of visna and bovine leukemia virus. Robert Strecker said that HIV was created in cell culture from bovine visna virus, and bovine visna virus in turn was created through a recombination of bovine leukemia virus with the sheep visna virus. Jakob Segal said that HIV was a recombinant of STLV-I and visna.

In the mid-80s when the various versions of the visna recombination theory were introduced, SIV had not yet been discovered. I don't know anyone before Nick who had said that HIV was a product of recombination between SIV and visna.

In the book "AIDS and the Doctors of Death", Alan Cantwell wrote: "I remember distinctly the early morning phone call from Dr. Virginia Livingston-Wheeler that started it all in August 1986." And he wrote she invited him to a meeting held by Robert Strecker, who "carefully explained the unique molecular composition of the AIDS virus, emphasizing how it greatly resembles a virus in sheep called 'visna virus.'"

In the same book, Cantwell wrote that the Strecker brothers had presented the visna theory in a report dated March 1986: "Strecker began to pass out copies of a report entitled 'This is a Bio-Attack Alert.' The manuscript was dated March 28, 1986, and was written by the Strecker brothers. The report contained detailed information on their theory, and provided documented evidence to show that the AIDS epidemic was biological warfare."

In the report, the Strecker brothers wrote: "The AIDS virus is Bovine Visna Virus (BVV) in man with a transacting transcriptional regulator gene inherited from Bovine Leukemia Virus". [https://strecker.pugik.com/bio-attack-alert/] "Bovine visna virus" was an early name for the bovine lentivirus that is now called BIV (bovine immunodeficiency virus), which is not the same species of virus as the visna-maedi virus in sheep. But Robert Strecker also claimed that "bovine visna virus" was created in a laboratory based on the sheep visna virus, so according to his nonsensical theory, HIV was still ultimately descended from the sheep visna virus.

In December 1985, the Los Angeles Times published an interview of John Seale that said: "Seale, who is in private practice and specializes in urinary and venereal diseases, said the AIDS virus, which attacks the body's immune system, has one gene extra over the Visna virus. [...] 'Inserting an extra gene into a virus is a routine procedure in modern genetic engineering,' Seale said." [https://www.latimes.com/archives/la-xpm-1985-12-19-mn-30652-story.html]

1. "Bovine visna virus" does not exist

  • Visna virus infects sheep (Ovis aries) and is a lentivirus (the same genus as HIV).
  • Bovine leukemia virus (BLV) infects cattle and is a deltaretrovirus, not a lentivirus.
  • There is no such natural or laboratory entity as "bovine visna virus."
  • The idea of "crossing" visna and BLV to make a "bovine visna" virus is scientifically incoherent: they are taxonomically and structurally too divergent to recombine naturally or even under laboratory conditions.

2. Inter-species viral recombination between such distant retroviruses is not viable

  • Retroviruses can recombine only when co-infecting the same cell and sharing closely related genomic structures.
  • BLV and visna virus have highly divergent genomes (~40-50% or less homology), use different receptors, and have different replication mechanisms.
  • They cannot "zip up" or recombine into a viable hybrid capable of replication or packaging.
  • Attempting such recombination would yield nonfunctional, replication-defective genomes.

3. The described "pseudovirus" method is misunderstood

  • "Pseudotyping" refers to packaging the genome of one virus inside the envelope proteins of another (e.g., vesicular stomatitis virus G-protein used for lentiviral vectors).
  • It does not alter the genome, nor does it cause recombination between unrelated viruses.
  • Pseudotyping works only when the core genome and packaging machinery are compatible, which is not the case for herpesviruses and retroviruses - these belong to completely different viral families (DNA vs. RNA viruses, different replication sites, and assembly pathways).
  • Hence, you cannot get a retrovirus genome inside a herpesvirus coat in any biologically meaningful or stable way.

4. Claims about "restriction enzyme cut sites" are misinterpreted

  • Restriction enzymes cut specific short DNA sequences - often at sites that occur naturally within genes.
  • The claim that HIV "falls apart exactly at the gene lines" is incorrect:
    • HIV's genome organization (gag-pol-env-tat-rev-nef, etc.) is continuous, and restriction sites are not aligned with gene boundaries.
    • In fact, natural HIV isolates show no evidence of artificial junctions or restriction signatures typical of engineered constructs.
    • Extensive sequencing of early HIV samples (from 1959, 1960, 1976) shows natural evolutionary continuity, not signs of synthetic assembly.

5. Growing viruses in human tissue does not "adapt" them across species barriers so easily

  • While viruses can adapt through serial passage, cross-species adaptation typically requires hundreds or thousands of generations and favorable receptor compatibility.
  • Visna virus cannot infect human cells because its entry receptors and host range restrictions are incompatible.
  • Simply "growing it in human tissue" would not produce a functional, replicating, human-tropic virus.

6. "Heat" or "detergent" treatment does not make cells "permeable to viruses"

  • Heating or treating cells with detergents kills or lyses them, destroying membranes and proteins essential for infection.

  • Injecting a cow with two unrelated viruses would produce no recombinant hybrid because:
    • The viruses infect different cell types and have incompatible replication cycles.
    • Even if both infected some common cell, the probability of viable recombination across genera is astronomically low.
