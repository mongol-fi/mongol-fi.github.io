Story of Sol Schnadow

The most famous book by John Loftus is The Belarus Secret, which was published in 1982. An expanded edition of the book was published in 2010 by Trine Day under the title America's Nazi Secret. The 1982 hardcover edition and 1989 paperback reissue of The Belarus Secret are both 196 pages long according to Amazon, but the 2010 Trine Day paperback is 336 pages long. [https://www.amazon.com/Belarus-Secret-John-J-Loftus/dp/0394522923/, https://www.amazon.com/Belarus-Secret-Nazi-Connection-America/dp/1557781389/, https://www.amazon.com/Americas-Nazi-Secret-Insiders-History/dp/1936296047/] I'm quoting from the 2010 Trine Day edition of the book below, because it mentions some details that were not mentioned in the original 1982 edition.

In America's Nazi Secret, Loftus wrote that after he was hired by the Office of Special Investigations, which was the Nazi hunting division of the DOJ, his "security clearances were upgraded above Top Secret (Q, SI, COSMIC)", and he wrote that at the OSI, "security clearances were raised - my own was three access levels above Top Secret - to permit inspection of the secret records of the various intelligence services". Loftus wrote:

When I joined OSI, my new boss discovered that I had some slight background in military intelligence as a former Army officer. Despite the fact that military intelligence is often regarded as a contradiction in terms, I was the first person in forty years to review the classified archives of U.S. intelligence. My job was to look in the old files for leads on any Nazis hiding in America.

I found more than I bargained for when I went to the archives in Suitland, a little town just outside Washington, D.C. That's where the Government's secrets are buried, literally. There are twenty vaults underground; each vault is one acre in size, crammed floor to ceiling with classified files. Do you remember the last scene from Raiders of the Lost Ark? That's what the vaults in Suitland are like, only not as organized. No one has the faintest idea what's down there. America had twenty-two different intelligence organizations after World War II, and the people who put the files away have long since retired.

I spent the better part of two years studying top-secret files in Suitland, and other archives like it.

Then Loftus wrote that he discovered the key document his book was based on, which were the memoirs of a Jew called Solomon Schiadow:

I spent weeks in underground vaults, learning the intricacies of long-forgotten intelligence operations, and then systematically retrieving the records from their dusty containers. It took years to reassemble all the documents, many of which had never even been translated. But there was one document that I could not forget: the memoir of Solomon Schiadow, who had survived the Byelorussian holocaust. Whenever I felt frustrated by a dead end or an uncooperative agency, I would remember what he had written, and feel renewed determination to keep on, sifting through the files for traces of the men who had committed the atrocities he recorded.

Sol's story was described in more detail in the book The Secret War Against the Jews, where his last name was written as "Schnadow" and not "Schiadow". In the book, Loftus and Aarons wrote that even though Sol's exploits were not recorded by history books, he was the only survivor of a Polish horseback invasion into Nazi Germany, and he was probably the first Jewish soldier to invade Nazi Germany. During his invasion, Sol supposedly went partially bald because his hair stuck to dried blood in his helmet (which does not seem physically plausible): [https://archive.org/details/secretwaragainst0000loft]

Three days after Germany attacked Poland with tanks and airplanes, Shnadow's unit counterattacked on horseback. The unit actually fought its way across the frontier and penetrated the Third Reich. Shnadow was probably the first Jewish soldier to invade Nazi Germany. The incident did not make it into the history books, however, because he was the only survivor. The German army caught the little band of Polish cavalrymen in an artillery ambush, followed by a machine gun attack. Shnadow's horse was blown to the ground, pinning him beneath it. In seconds the battle was over. The brave Polish cavalry surrendered. Then the German infantry moved in to take its revenge.

First, the Germans shot the prisoners of war and then the Polish wounded. Shnadow could hear the footsteps as the executioners approached. He could only lie there helplessly and close his eyes as the shots came closer. A German soldier looked down at him, covered from head to toe with the blood of his horse. "This one's dead," the soldier muttered, and moved on. For seven hours Shnadow lay still amid the corpses, waiting for the Germans to finish their killing and leave. When darkness came, he struggled from beneath the horse and threw off his helmet. To his amazement, all the hair on the top of his head was stuck to the dried blood in the helmet. He was alive, but partially bald.

Shnadow changed his uniform for some clothes he had stolen and snuck back across the border into Poland, the only survivor of the first raid on the Third Reich.

Apparently Sol's hair never grew back, because a few pages later when the authors described events that took place several years later in 1945, the authors wrote that Sol managed to avoid capture by the Soviets because of his baldness: "The soldiers ran right past. The Soviets were looking for a young soldier with hair, not a balding refugee. Making him lose his hair was the only favor the Nazis had done for him."

According to Loftus and Aarons, the main person responsible for the Belarusian Holocaust was Stanislaw Stankievich, who invented a novel "sardine method" which economically enabled killing a double layer of Jews with one bullet:

The SS had asked whether Stankievich could massacre every one of the eight thousand Jews in Borisov in a single day, without any German help. Stankievich thought it over and said that it could be done. He threw a wild party for his police force, to get them properly drunk for the work that was to come.

The raid began at 3:00 A.M. on October 20. It was all very systematic. The Jews were shuttled out one hundred at a time to a road near the airport where long ditches had been dug. There they were forced to undress and made to lie on top of each other, head to toe. Stankievich's idea was to shoot through a double row of bodies to save ammunition. It was his own unique contribution to the horrors of the Holocaust. Of course, if the Jew on top was shot through the heart, the one on the bottom was only wounded. The police were told to cover the wounded with a thin layer of dirt and then suffocate them beneath another double layer of bodies. It went on all day: a layer of dirt, a double layer of bodies, another layer of dirt, and so on. Stankievich was very proud of his system. He called it the "sardine method."

After the war the worst part of the atrocities was discovered: The bodies of the smallest children had no wounds. Apparently, to save the price of a bullet, Stankievich buried the babies alive.

The Holocaust literature is full of stories of crafty Jews who managed to escape from a gas chamber, so in similar fashion, Loftus told that some of the Belarusian Jews managed to cleverly dig themselves out of mass graves: "The mass graves were overfilled, and some of the victims, who had only been wounded, dug themselves out." However the clumsiness of the "sardine method" appears to have inspired the invention of gas chambers: "The massacres nauseated the Germans because the methods were considered clumsy. There were complaints back to Berlin about wounded Jews crawling out of graves alive. [...] Eichmann's diary noted that he was revolted by the brutal method of killing in the Belarus region and ordered experiments with poison gas as a more 'humane' alternative." Loftus didn't cite any source for Eichmann's diary, because no such diary entry exists. Loftus and Aarons also wrote: "Because the gas vans and the liquidation squad simply could not keep up with the huge numbers of Jews to be killed, Eichmann conceived of poison gas as an alternative. Partly in revulsion against the cruel methods of Stankievich and his colleagues, and partly as an efficiency measure, he brought the gas chamber into use."

Out of about 4,000 Jews in his town, Sol is supposed to have been the only survivor, who was spared because he falsely said he was a bricklayer, and the Nazis needed bricklayers to work at a concentration camp. When Sol was at the concentration camp, "the young White Russian trainees tried to outdo each other in cruelty", so that "A baby was thrown in the air and caught with a pitchfork. A young man was tied to a horse, drenched with kerosene, and then he and the animal were burned alive. One guard took a butcher knife and carved out human organs while the victim was still alive."

According to Loftus and Aarons, Sol then led the escape of 125 Jews from the concentration camp:

Shnadow and four others decided to plan a breakout. It was madness, but there was no other choice. Shnadow said that if even one of them lived to be a witness, to tell what the Nazis had done, it would be worth it. The last 125 Jews were marked for death anyway. Under the noses of the SS, they dug a tunnel out of the stable leading to the fence. The blacksmith's shop made a pair of wire cutters. Time was running out. Soon the fence would be electrified.

That night Shnadow led the breakout. His job was to kill the closest guard and cut the wires. But the guard already was dead drunk, and the heavy snowfall muffled their escape. In one of the worst blizzards of the season, the last Jews in the camp made their escape into the night. It was dawn before the guards realized what had happened. The Germans thought it a poor reflection on White Russian efficiency that Jews could escape from a school for camp guards. Stankievich ordered a massive manhunt and captured one of the inmates. Before he was tortured to death, he revealed who had planned the breakout. Stankievich put a price of 10,000 marks on Shnadow's head.

In America's Nazi Secret, Loftus wrote that Sol himself dug the tunnel: "This little Jewish barber dug a tunnel under a Nazi concentration camp and led the last hundred Jews in a breakout. The Nazis put out a reward of 10,000 marks on Sol's head."

Loftus and Aarons wrote that afterwards when Sol had moved to the United States, he happened to give a copy of his memoirs to the FBI, but the memoirs remained buried in the FBI's archives until Loftus discovered the memoirs in 1979:

Years passed, and then one day the FBI visited Shadow and showed him a photograph of an alleged White Russian war criminal. Shadow gave the FBI agents a copy of his memoirs, but told them he was quite sure that the man in the photo had never been in Koldychevo. It was all rather puzzling. Shnadow went back to work at his barbershop and thought no more about the visit.

Shnadow's story was hidden for many years, until one of the authors found a copy of his memoirs in a classified FBI file. In 1979, John Loftus was one the first attorneys assigned to the Justice Department's Nazi hunting unit, the Office of Special Investigations. By luck of the draw, he had been assigned to review the White Russian cases, the crimes of men from Belarus.

When I pasted the text to Grok and asked if it is possible for Borrelia anserina to become Borrelia burgdorferi, Grok replied:

No, it is not possible for Borrelia anserina to "become" Borrelia burgdorferi. These are two distinct bacterial species within the genus Borrelia, and the claim that one transformed or evolved directly into the other - particularly through work by Erich Traub or Werner Schäfer involving weaponization or lab manipulation - is not supported by scientific evidence.

Borrelia anserina (formerly known as Spirochaeta gallinarum) causes avian spirochetosis, a disease primarily affecting birds (e.g., poultry like chickens and geese). It is transmitted by argasid (soft) ticks and has been known since the late 19th century.

Borrelia burgdorferi (more precisely Borrelia burgdorferi sensu stricto, with related species in the B. burgdorferi sensu lato complex) causes Lyme disease in humans and other mammals. It is transmitted by hard ticks (primarily Ixodes species) and was first identified as a distinct pathogen in the early 1980s by Willy Burgdorfer.

The 1989 paper titled "Shared flagellar epitopes of Borrelia burgdorferi and Borrelia anserina" (published in Veterinary Microbiology) demonstrates antigenic cross-reactivity - specifically, the two species share at least two epitopes on their flagella. One of these shared epitopes is not found in other Borrelia species like B. hermsii or B. coriaceae. This is based on studies using mouse immune sera and monoclonal antibodies in immunoblots.

This shared antigenicity reflects evolutionary relatedness within the Borrelia genus (both are spirochetes), but it does not mean one species can convert into the other, nor does it indicate genetic transformation, mutation under lab conditions, or weaponization leading to Lyme disease. Flagellar proteins (like the 41-kDa flagellin) are conserved across many spirochetes, leading to cross-reactions in diagnostics, but the bacteria remain separate species with different hosts, vectors, diseases, and genomic profiles.

The quoted passage about Schäfer maintaining Spirochaeta gallinarum (i.e., B. anserina) in chick embryos for research and teaching purposes aligns with historical veterinary microbiology practices - chick embryos were a common, inexpensive method to propagate certain pathogens before modern cell culture techniques. There is no credible evidence linking this to creating or modifying a Lyme disease agent.

Grok said that the time to the most recent common ancestor of anserina and burgdorferi is estimated to be millions of years ago, so it wouldn't have been possible to create burgdorferi from anserina through serial passage within a time span of a few years, and the diversity between different strains of Borrelia burgdorferi is so great that the strains cannot have had a common ancestor less than a hundred years ago:

The genetic distance between Borrelia burgdorferi (the primary agent of Lyme disease) and Borrelia anserina (the agent of avian spirochetosis) is substantial, reflecting their placement in separate major clades within the Borrelia genus: Lyme disease-associated (B. burgdorferi sensu lato, or LB group) versus relapsing fever-associated (RF group, where B. anserina resides as a classic avian/soft-tick transmitted species).

  • 16S rRNA gene sequences (commonly used for bacterial phylogeny) show clear divergence. Studies from the early 1990s (e.g., Marconi et al., 1992) aligned B. burgdorferi sequences with those of B. anserina, B. hermsii, and others, revealing species-level differences sufficient to distinguish them phylogenetically. Typical 16S rRNA similarity between LB and RF Borrelia species is around 95-97% (implying ~3-5% divergence), though exact pairwise values for these two are not uniformly reported in summaries - distance matrices place B. anserina outside the LB cluster with significant branch lengths.

  • Whole-genome and multi-locus comparisons indicate even greater divergence. The LB group (B. burgdorferi s.l.) and RF group (including B. anserina) form monophyletic sister lineages that diverged from a common ancestor long ago. Some proposals suggest splitting them into separate genera due to genomic differences (e.g., chromosome structure, plasmid content, gene presence/absence like glpQ in RF but absent in LB). Average nucleotide identity (ANI) or similar metrics across genomes would likely fall well below the ~95-96% threshold for species delineation.

  • Time to most recent common ancestor (TMRCA) of B. burgdorferi and B. anserina is not precisely dated in available literature for this specific pair, but broader Borrelia phylogenies and divergence estimates place the split between LB and RF clades in the distant past - likely millions of years ago, predating modern mammal-tick cycles. For context, diversity within B. burgdorferi s.l. itself dates back 60,000 years or more in some North American lineages, while transitions (e.g., to louse-borne RF) are more recent (8,000 years in some cases). The LB-RF divergence is far older, consistent with deep phylogenetic separation.

So if B. burgdorferi was created from B. anserina by Traub, then were all of the other species of Borrelia in the LB group also created from B. anserina by Traub?

A 1% divergence in the 16S gene is considered to typically correspond to about 50 million years of evolution in free-living bacteria, or about 1-2 million years in endosymbionts. [https://www.pnas.org/doi/full/10.1073/pnas.96.22.12638] Pathogenic bacteria often have a rate of evolution that is intermediate between free-living bacteria and endosymbionts, even though I don't know if that's the case for Borrelia. But regardless, a 4% divergence in 16S between species of Borrelia would likely correspond to tens or hundreds of millions of years of evolution.

So did Traub find some way to accelerate evolution by around 7 to 8 orders of magnitude, so that within a period of only a few years, he was able to create all the different species in the LB group by serial passage starting from Borrelia anserina?

In the introduction to Finnegan's book, Loftus suggested that Borrelia burgdorferi was created through serial passage, and he wrote that "serial passages can achieve laboratory viral and bacterial mutations in a few weeks that would take decades to develop in nature". [https://play.google.com/books/reader?id=aTKdEQAAQBAJ&pg=GBS.PT11.w.0.4.13_207] But the difference between decades and a few weeks is only about 2 to 3 orders of magnitude, and not 7 to 8 orders of magnitude.

The term Borrelia burgdorferi is sometimes employed in a broad sense (sensu lato), which refers to a group of species of Borrelia associated with Lyme borreliosis, but the term is more often employed in a strict sense (sensu stricto), which only includes a single species within the group. The broad sense is synonymous with the LB group of Borrelia species.

A study from 2024 said that the "mean age of the last common ancestor of all Bbsl species is estimated to be 117 ± 58 million years (Myr)" (where "Bbsl" is short for Borrelia burgdorferi sensu lato, which does not include Borrelia anserina). [https://journals.asm.org/doi/10.1128/mbio.01749-24]

In one study the most recent common ancestor of North American strains of Borrelia burgdorferi sensu stricto was dated to about 60,000 years ago. [https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6431794/] So it's not possible for the strains to be descended from a single ancestral strain that was created less than a hundred years ago by Erich Traub.

When I asked Grok if what Finnegan wrote was true, Grok said:

No, the statement is not true. It overstates the evidence on the relationship between Borrelia burgdorferi (the main Lyme disease agent) and Borrelia anserina (an avian relapsing fever spirochete).

The 1989 paper by Walker et al. shows shared flagellar epitopes between B. burgdorferi and B. anserina, reflecting conserved flagellin proteins common across spirochetes. This indicates antigenic cross-reactivity due to distant common ancestry at the genus level, not close phylogenetic ties or derivation of one from the other.

The 1995 paper by Hovind-Hougen describes the morphology of B. anserina but makes no phylogenetic comparison to Lyme agents (B. burgdorferi, B. garinii, B. afzelii) and does not suggest they are closely related.

Modern phylogenetics (using 16S rRNA, flaB, multilocus typing, and genomes) places the Lyme borreliosis (LB) group in one monophyletic clade and the relapsing fever group (RFG, including B. anserina) in a separate sister clade. B. anserina belongs firmly to the RFG (soft-tick transmitted, avian-specific) and is not basal to or the "parent" of the LB group. Hard-tick species like B. miyamotoi are closer to LB than B. anserina.

The cited papers 84 and 85 do not support a close relationship. Marconi et al. (1995) analyzes divergence within Lyme-related borreliae and mentions no B. anserina. Fukunaga et al. (1996) uses flaB sequences to separate Lyme borreliae from relapsing fever/avian species like B. anserina, reinforcing distinct lineages.

Birds serving as reservoirs for B. burgdorferi (via hard ticks like Ixodes) reflects ecological overlap, not evolutionary origin from B. anserina (soft-tick transmitted fowl spirochetosis agent). No credible evidence positions B. anserina as the closest relative or ancestor of Lyme disease agents.

The divergence time t can be calculated as t=K/μ/2, where K is the number of substitutions per site with Jukes-Cantor correction, and μ is the rate of substitutions per site per year. In one paper the mutation rate of Borrelia burgdorferi was estimated to be on the order of 1e−9 substitutions per site per year. [https://www.pnas.org/doi/10.1073/pnas.0903810106] The K parameter for a similarity percent of 75% is -3/4*ln(1-4/3*(1-.75)), which is about 0.304, and 0.304/1e-9/2 gives a divergence time of about 150 million years.

In 2013 when someone tried to do two FOIA requests to the CIA for files about Blome including the RG 330 file, both times the CIA replied: "The request has been rejected, with the agency stating that it can neither confirm nor deny the existence of the requested documents." [https://www.muckrock.com/foi/united-states-of-america-10/foia-request-regarding-kurt-blome-files-8638/] If Finnegan has access to the RG 330 documents, he should upload them somewhere online like at archive.org. Under US law, documents created by the federal government are not eligible for copyright and they are automatically in the public domain.
