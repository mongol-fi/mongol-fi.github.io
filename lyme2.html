Erich Traub's research on tick-borne pathogens

1. 1939 - Learning about tick-borne diseases from Karl Beller

In 1939, while serving as assistant to Karl Beller at the University of Giessen, Traub allegedly received instruction on various tick-borne diseases. Finnegan claims Beller taught him about Rickettsia, Babesia, Bartonella, Ehrlichia, and the specific ticks capable of transmitting them. Finnegan interprets this exposure as foundational preparation for Traub's later supposed use of these agents in weaponized ticks released on the West.

2. 1939 - Access to avian spirochete (Borrelia anserina)

In 1939, Werner Schäfer, described as Traub's associate assistant and protégé at the University of Giessen, published a paper on maintaining avian spirochetes. The cited work is Schäfer 1939 - "The Maintenance of Spirochaeta gallinarum by Passages in Fowl Embryos." Schäfer reportedly received the strain from Dr. Franz Jahnel, and Finnegan states that Traub and Beller were Schäfer's superiors at the institute. The paper itself describes a practical method for keeping fully virulent strains alive in embryonated eggs instead of live chickens for teaching and research purposes. Finnegan interprets this as evidence that Traub came into possession of the avian spirochete (Spirochaeta gallinarum, later renamed Borrelia anserina), which he claims was later weaponized into Borrelia burgdorferi.

3. 1941 - Mention of blood parasites in the horse respiratory catarrh paper

In 1941, during an investigation of a respiratory outbreak in German Army horses belonging to a veterinary company, Traub and his colleagues reportedly conducted experiments that included checks for blood parasites along with equine infectious anemia and infectious respiratory catarrh. The paper is titled "Infectious Respiratory Catarrh in the Horses of a Veterinary Company" (Traub 1941). Finnegan highlights the inclusion of blood-parasite examinations as evidence of Traub's direct laboratory experience with such organisms. He links this work to possible familiarity with agents like Babesia, a common equine blood parasite, and suggests the study of mixed infections could have been useful for creating diagnostically confusing disease presentations.

4. 1953 USDA poultry conference listing avian spirochetosis as a biological warfare concern

In 1953, a USDA poultry conference on biological warfare threats listed avian spirochetosis (Borrelia anserina) among "special agents" of particular concern. Finnegan claims this listing proves that Traub's earlier work with the avian spirochete at Giessen was recognized as a potential bioweapon. He further asserts that this conference provides evidence that Lyme disease (Borrelia burgdorferi) originated from Traub's alleged weaponization of the avian strain, noting the complete absence of subsequent Plum Island research on Borrelia anserina despite the lab's mandate to study foreign animal diseases with zoonotic potential.

5. Vague references to Traub's 1950s U.S. Navy work on pathogens and rickettsia

During his employment at the Naval Medical Research Institute in the early 1950s, Traub was assigned as Supervisory Bacteriologist with responsibilities connected to maintaining approximately 40 strains of viruses and rickettsiae. Finnegan interprets this broad mandate as including tick-borne diseases, even though no specific tick-borne agents are named in the surviving job descriptions or publications from that period. He presents these duties as circumstantial support for the idea that Traub continued working with or supervising research on rickettsial and tick-related pathogens under U.S. Navy auspices.

6. Narrative ties in Traub's later papers on immune tolerance and mixed infections

In Traub's post-war publications on immune tolerance, lymphocytic choriomeningitis virus (LCM), Eastern equine encephalomyelitis (EEE), and related topics, Finnegan occasionally draws narrative connections to "stealth" or mixed/co-infections. When I pasted the text to Grok and asked if it is possible for Borrelia anserina to become Borrelia burgdorferi, Grok replied:

No, it is not possible for Borrelia anserina to "become" Borrelia burgdorferi. These are two distinct bacterial species within the genus Borrelia, and the claim that one transformed or evolved directly into the other - particularly through work by Erich Traub or Werner Schäfer involving weaponization or lab manipulation - is not supported by scientific evidence.

Borrelia anserina (formerly known as Spirochaeta gallinarum) causes avian spirochetosis, a disease primarily affecting birds (e.g., poultry like chickens and geese). It is transmitted by argasid (soft) ticks and has been known since the late 19th century.

Borrelia burgdorferi (more precisely Borrelia burgdorferi sensu stricto, with related species in the B. burgdorferi sensu lato complex) causes Lyme disease in humans and other mammals. It is transmitted by hard ticks (primarily Ixodes species) and was first identified as a distinct pathogen in the early 1980s by Willy Burgdorfer.

The 1989 paper titled "Shared flagellar epitopes of Borrelia burgdorferi and Borrelia anserina" (published in Veterinary Microbiology) demonstrates antigenic cross-reactivity - specifically, the two species share at least two epitopes on their flagella. One of these shared epitopes is not found in other Borrelia species like B. hermsii or B. coriaceae. This is based on studies using mouse immune sera and monoclonal antibodies in immunoblots.

This shared antigenicity reflects evolutionary relatedness within the Borrelia genus (both are spirochetes), but it does not mean one species can convert into the other, nor does it indicate genetic transformation, mutation under lab conditions, or weaponization leading to Lyme disease. Flagellar proteins (like the 41-kDa flagellin) are conserved across many spirochetes, leading to cross-reactions in diagnostics, but the bacteria remain separate species with different hosts, vectors, diseases, and genomic profiles.

The quoted passage about Schäfer maintaining Spirochaeta gallinarum (i.e., B. anserina) in chick embryos for research and teaching purposes aligns with historical veterinary microbiology practices - chick embryos were a common, inexpensive method to propagate certain pathogens before modern cell culture techniques. There is no credible evidence linking this to creating or modifying a Lyme disease agent.

Grok said that the time to the most recent common ancestor of anserina and burgdorferi is estimated to be millions of years ago, so it wouldn't have been possible to create burgdorferi from anserina through serial passage within a time span of a few years, and the diversity between different strains of Borrelia burgdorferi is so great that the strains cannot have had a common ancestor less than a hundred years ago:

The genetic distance between Borrelia burgdorferi (the primary agent of Lyme disease) and Borrelia anserina (the agent of avian spirochetosis) is substantial, reflecting their placement in separate major clades within the Borrelia genus: Lyme disease-associated (B. burgdorferi sensu lato, or LB group) versus relapsing fever-associated (RF group, where B. anserina resides as a classic avian/soft-tick transmitted species).

  • 16S rRNA gene sequences (commonly used for bacterial phylogeny) show clear divergence. Studies from the early 1990s (e.g., Marconi et al., 1992) aligned B. burgdorferi sequences with those of B. anserina, B. hermsii, and others, revealing species-level differences sufficient to distinguish them phylogenetically. Typical 16S rRNA similarity between LB and RF Borrelia species is around 95-97% (implying ~3-5% divergence), though exact pairwise values for these two are not uniformly reported in summaries - distance matrices place B. anserina outside the LB cluster with significant branch lengths.

  • Whole-genome and multi-locus comparisons indicate even greater divergence. The LB group (B. burgdorferi s.l.) and RF group (including B. anserina) form monophyletic sister lineages that diverged from a common ancestor long ago. Some proposals suggest splitting them into separate genera due to genomic differences (e.g., chromosome structure, plasmid content, gene presence/absence like glpQ in RF but absent in LB). Average nucleotide identity (ANI) or similar metrics across genomes would likely fall well below the ~95-96% threshold for species delineation.

  • Time to most recent common ancestor (TMRCA) of B. burgdorferi and B. anserina is not precisely dated in available literature for this specific pair, but broader Borrelia phylogenies and divergence estimates place the split between LB and RF clades in the distant past - likely millions of years ago, predating modern mammal-tick cycles. For context, diversity within B. burgdorferi s.l. itself dates back 60,000 years or more in some North American lineages, while transitions (e.g., to louse-borne RF) are more recent (8,000 years in some cases). The LB-RF divergence is far older, consistent with deep phylogenetic separation.

The divergence time t can be calculated as t=K/μ/2, where K is the number of substitutions per site with Jukes-Cantor correction, and μ is the rate of substitutions per site per year. In one paper the mutation rate of Borrelia burgdorferi was estimated to be on the order of 1e−9 substitutions per site per year. [https://www.pnas.org/doi/10.1073/pnas.0903810106] The K parameter for a similarity percent of 75% is -3/4*ln(1-4/3*(1-.75)), which is about 0.304, and 0.304/1e-9/2 gives a divergence time of about 150 million years.

No, I don't agree. The idea that the entire Lyme borreliosis clade (Borrelia burgdorferi sensu lato, or LB group, including species like B. burgdorferi s.s., B. afzelii, B. garinii, etc.) rapidly derived from Borrelia anserina (an avian relapsing-fever spirochete) in just a few years under lab-driven "biological arms race" conditions is not supported by the scientific evidence on Borrelia phylogeny, genomics, or evolutionary rates. It's a creative hypothesis from the Substack piece (which frames Lyme as engineered starting ~1939 via German labs like Insel Riems, serial egg passages, co-infections, phages/mycoplasma as gene shuttles, and later Plum Island work under Erich Traub), but it requires rejecting core molecular data in favor of circumstantial historical overlaps. Here's why it doesn't hold up.

1. Phylogeny shows deep, ancient divergence - not recent derivation

  • B. anserina is the type species of the genus Borrelia and sits firmly in the relapsing-fever (RF) clade (along with agents like B. hermsii, B. duttonii, etc.). The Lyme borreliosis group forms a separate, monophyletic clade (sometimes reclassified as Borreliella) that is sister to the RF group but clearly distinct.

  • Multiple independent studies (using 16S rRNA, flaB, multi-locus sequence typing/MLST, whole-chromosome SNPs, conserved housekeeping genes, and full genomes) consistently root the LB tree with RF species (including B. anserina or close relatives) as the outgroup. LB species cluster together with their own internal structure; there's no evidence of LB "branching off" from B. anserina itself.

  • Morphological similarities or shared epitopes (e.g., flagellar proteins) noted in older papers are superficial and expected in related but divergent spirochetes - they don't override genomic phylogeny. Horizontal gene transfer (HGT) via phages does occur in Borrelia, but it doesn't rewrite the core chromosomal backbone enough to collapse a deep clade split into a recent event. Modern phylogenomics (hundreds of single-copy orthologs) confirms the split is real and ancient.

2. Divergence times and diversity are incompatible with a 1930s lab origin

  • Genomic dating puts the most recent common ancestor (MRCA) of B. burgdorferi s.s. (the main North American Lyme agent) at roughly ~60,000 years ago (with credible intervals placing it well before the Last Glacial Maximum ~20,000 years ago). Diversity is ancient, geographically structured, and shows pre-human migration patterns across North America - long predating any 20th-century labs.

  • The broader LB clade shows even deeper roots, with species radiations tied to host/vector adaptations over tens of thousands of years (e.g., bird vs. mammal specialists like B. garinii vs. B. bavariensis). This matches natural phylogeography, not a sudden post-1939 "starburst" radiation from one lab strain.

  • Even within the RF group, divergences (e.g., louse-borne B. recurrentis from its tick-borne ancestor) are estimated at ~4,000-8,000 years ago - still orders of magnitude older than "several years." A lab-driven jump from RF (B. anserina) to a whole new LB clade in the 1930s-1940s would require implausibly fast, coordinated changes across the chromosome, plasmids, and regulatory networks while leaving no detectable "lab signature" in modern genomes.

3. Biology and lab realities make the rapid "arms race" transformation implausible

  • B. anserina is adapted to birds, soft ticks (argasid), and primarily causes acute septicemic disease with transovarial transmission. Lyme agents are tissue-tropic, use hard ticks (ixodid), persist chronically in mammals, and have a very different plasmid complement (e.g., OspA/OspC surface proteins, cp26, lp54, etc.). Switching vectors, hosts, tropisms, and disease patterns at that scale in a few years - even with aggressive serial passaging, co-infections, or "Russian-doll" cocktails - would be unprecedented in bacterial evolution. Lab evolution can drive fast adaptation (e.g., antibiotic resistance or host jumps in controlled settings), but not the creation of an entire ecologically distinct clade that perfectly mimics millions of years of natural divergence.

  • The global biowarfare context (Nazi tick research, Paperclip transfers, Plum Island releases) is real and worth studying - ticks and borreliae were indeed investigated as vectors/simulants. But no declassified records, genomic forensics, or epidemiological patterns support B. anserina being the direct precursor to Lyme borreliae. Lyme's emergence in the 1970s aligns better with ecological changes (reforestation, deer/tick population booms, suburban sprawl) acting on a pre-existing natural pathogen.

The Substack piece rightly notes that pre-1939 B. anserina sequences are limited and that phylogeny isn't "absolute" under extreme human influence - but the sheer volume of independent genomic datasets (from wild ticks, ancient samples, global isolates) all converge on the same ancient natural history. Dismissing that as universally skewed by HGT or manipulation would require extraordinary evidence, which isn't presented.
