Origin of linear particle accelerator story

The earliest reference I found to the story that the SV40 virus was weaponized by beaming it with a linear particle accelerator was in Ed Haslam's 1995 book Mary, Ferrie & The Monkey Virus. In the book Haslam claimed that he heard about the linear particle accelerator from his physics teacher in high school:

I had fairly good personal information that there was at least one linear accelerator in New Orleans in the 1960s: The Jesuit priest who taught physics in 1968 confided to our class that there was a linear accelerator being used 'for research' at a medical facility in New Orleans. And I think that it is reasonable to suggest that a doctor with Mary Sherman's reputation for researching treatments for bone cancers may have had access to it. But the question is: Did she?

In the original 1995 book, Haslam merely speculated that Mary Sherman might have had access to a linear particle accelerator, but he didn't yet claim to know where the accelerator was located. He wrote:

Did Mary Sherman have access to high energy radiation equipment? We should note what equipment was being put into medical facilities at the time. In 1959 Time magazine ran a cover article called "The New War on Cancer via Virus Research & Chemotherapy." In it, we read "Almost daily, ways are found to give bigger radiation doses more safely to hard-to-reach parts of the body." Their list of techniques included radioisotopes (cobalt-60, iridium 192, and yttrium 90), higher-powered X-ray machines and linear particle accelerators.[34] Was there a linear particle accelerator at one of the facilities where Mary Sherman worked? And if so, did she have access to it?

The real problem here is that the smallest viruses like SIV (and now HIV) are so small that they are even hard to kill with ionizing radiation.[35]

So what happens if you hit one, but don't kill it? What happens if you wound it with a stream of sub-atomic particles ripping through its strands of genetic information, mangling its molecules and scrambling its sequence? If you do, and if it is still capable of breeding, you now have a mutant. A new virus. One that behaves differently from the one you just mangled. It may be more virulent; it may be less. It may even behave differently from all other known viruses, since they evolved naturally and this new virus did not. This is the real danger. The moment you place a test tube full of viruses in front of a linear-particle accelerator, you enter a brave new world. And you become part of the biological history of our planet.

Was Mary Sherman using a linear particle accelerator to kill or weaken monkey viruses as part of a desperate attempt to develop an anti-cancer vaccine? Was she testing the results of those experiments in live animals in Ferrie's underground medical laboratory? Is this how good science goes bad?

Then he pointed out that Mary Sherman's autopsy report said that only a burned stump was left of her right arm, and a piece of her right rib cage was missing, and he speculated that the injuries couldn't have resulted from an ordinary fire. And then he wrote:

Finally it all came together for me. A scenario that explained all of the mysterious elements of the case in a logical manner. Here is how I would explain the events related to Mary's death.

On Monday Mary was going to work in a laboratory. Due to the unusual nature of this work, the normal day time traffic of doctors, patients, and staff would not be welcome. This work had to be done at night. Since she would be working late that night, she took Monday afternoon off to go to the dentist and to run an errand. In the morning she would be going to the Crippled Children's Hospital across the lake. The children were always happy to see her, even though they were the ones who suffered. She wanted to look happy when she saw them. It was one of the private joys of a childless widow. She laid out her festive black and white polka dot dress for her visit to see the children in the morning, not for a trip to the lab that evening. Then she washed her hair early, around 4:00 P.M., because she would not have time to do it later. She told her housekeeper, who was curious as to why she was washing her hair in the afternoon, that she was expecting visitors from out of town. A white lie, as it's called. It would not be proper to say that she was going to work on a secret medical project at the lab. After her housekeeper left, she slipped into some functional clothes and headed for the lab. There maybe she would finally find the elusive cancer remedy which she sought. Maybe tonight she would finally be able to neutralize the infinitesimal culprit that killed both children and adults so slowly and so painfully. Maybe this would be the step that made her dream of a cancer vaccine possible. The new machine had great potential. And it had been put in her hands. She was now standing on the leading edge of science. Her week of training in Boston would pay off now. She went to the lab with high hopes.

That night there was a terrible accident in the lab. It all happened in a brilliant flash of light. She had just completed irradiating a vile of blood and was going to remove the test tube from its holder. Without warning an electrical arc of unbelievable magnitude leapt from the machine and struck Mary in the arm. A virtual fire ball of overcharged particles flowed from the multi-million watt machine. A boulder of pure electricity struck her, beyond comprehension in its power. In an instant the massive electrical force lunged through her body, its intense heat and voltage literally blew her arm off and demolished the right side of her body instantaneously. Her body was destroyed almost beyond recognition by a blast of electromagnetic energy that lasted barely a fraction of a second. A flashbulb of violence. She never knew what hit her.

[And so on...]

So Haslam came up with an elaborate hypothetical scenario of how Mary Sherman died, and he even placed his story about the scenario within a blockquote. He made up fictional dates for his scenario, and he even came up with fictional feelings the characters in his scenario experienced. But he presented little evidence that his scenario had actually occurred.

Then he proceeded to write about "his guess" of what "probably" happened: "What happened in the lab that night? My guess is that Dr. Sherman was conducting leading-edge cancer experiments which were being kept extremely secret. This is why the work was being done at night. These experiments probably involved the use of a linear accelerator to kill or mutate known carcinogenic monkey viruses for the purpose of developing an anti-cancer vaccine. The results of these viral experiments were probably being tested in live animals in the underground medical laboratory which David Ferrie ran. Simply said, the risk of exposing an operation of these dimensions mandated Dr. Sherman's euthanasic termination and the subsequent obstruction of justice. But who sent the investigation on a wild goose chase, looking for a psychopathic lesbian butcher that never existed?"

Dr. X, Mr. Y, and Mr. Z

In the 2007 book Dr. Mary's Monkey, Ed Haslam introduced 4 new eyewitnesses to his story who were not featured in the 1995 book, who were Dr. X, Mr. Y, Mr. Z, and Judyth Vary Baker. All of them now corroborated his earlier hypothesis about the linear particle accelerator.

Supposedly Dr. X was a doctor who operated the linear particle accelerator in the New Orleans hospital, Mr. Y was the sales director of the company that manufactured the accelerator, and Mr. Z was the building manager of the building that housed the accelerator.

Haslam wrote:

Six months passed with little change. Then the phone rang. It was a cold winter night in January 1996. The voice was warm and familiar. It was a medical doctor who had quietly fed me information over the past several years. His kiss-and-tell stories about radioactive medicine and medical politics had encouraged me at a time when little else did. I will call him Dr. X, for reasons that will become obvious shortly. We had spoken often, but not in recent months. During those earlier phone calls, he frequently talked about his long career and detailed many of the people and places he knew along the way. Of particular interest to me was his experience with linear particle accelerators.

As a young surgeon in the early 1960s, Dr. X had worked at a well-known cancer clinic on the East Coast. Operating on cancer patients was his business. Day after day he removed tumors and repaired organs with varying degrees of success. The daily grind was an excruciating battle between life and death. As times changed, new technology brought new hope. Chemotherapy and radiation therapy offered alternatives to radical surgery, and brought new promises to both doctors and cancer patients.

Radioactive substances, such as Cobalt-60, were injected into patients in hope of destroying their tumors. It was a desperate hope. The side effects were often terrible. None of the medical staff liked the idea of injecting patients with radioactive substances strong enough to destroy living tissue. Nor did they like the idea of standing by watching countless patients die. They all hoped that things would get better.

Considering these circumstances, it was not surprising that Dr. X and his colleagues welcomed the introduction of the linear particle accelerator as a new, improved means of destroying cancer tumors. The accelerator's main advantage was that the direction of the radioactive beam could be controlled, aimed precisely at the tumor, rather than emitting radiation in all directions and into the surrounding tissue as Cobalt had done. Fewer healthy cells would be destroyed, and less radiation would penetrate the bloodstream. It was an improvement at least, and it offered new hope. The hospital spent millions of dollars on this new technology and renovated a building to house the huge machine, which Dr. X came to use on a regular basis.

In this setting Dr. X came to know the people who designed and built his linear particle accelerator. One was Mr. Y, the manufacturer's Director of Sales, who had sold the machine and who serviced the account. Mr. Y spent so much time at Dr. X's hospital that he rented an apartment nearby. Dr. X and Mr. Y became friends, as well as professional colleagues. At one point Dr. X sublet a room in Mr. Y's apartment, so for a time they were roommates.

Mr. Y was a colorful character, a Peter Paul and Mary vintage nonconformist with a Ph.D. in physics from Harvard. During the time Dr. X knew him, Mr. Y dated a beautiful French woman who danced with the Rockettes at Radio City Music Hall. According to Dr. X, Mr. Y's success was based both upon his brilliant mind and his father's close association with Harvard University's Board of Directors.[2] Dr. X gave me all the relevant details which I have stored safely.

There are probably not too many people who have the combination of attributes that they were the director of sales of a company that manufactured linear particle accelerators, that they dated a French woman who danced with the Rockettes, and that their father had a close association with the Harvard board of directors. The last two details were not even relevant to the story.

By this time, Mr. Y and his linear particle accelerators were a familiar subject to me. He had built about 10 accelerators around the world. Each one was uniquely designed for a special application. From Israel to South Africa to New Orleans, Mr. Y shepherded the design, sales and installation of some of the world's most mysterious machines. Linear particle accelerator hypothesis

Grok said:

No, there is no documented evidence that a linear particle accelerator (linac) has been used specifically to accelerate the mutation rate in a virus study. Linacs have supported virus research through structural imaging (via X-ray production) and inactivation/sterilization experiments, but not targeted mutagenesis of viruses.

Radiation can induce mutations in viruses by damaging their nucleic acids, but linacs are rarely chosen for this purpose due to their high cost, large size, and complexity. Easier and more common methods exist:

  • UV radiation is the simplest and most accessible way to accelerate viral mutation rates. It causes specific DNA/RNA lesions (like thymine dimers) that lead to errors during replication if not repaired. Labs routinely use UV lamps for this in virology experiments.

  • Gamma rays from cobalt-60 sources or low-energy X-rays from standard lab irradiators are widely used for radiation-induced mutagenesis in microbes and viruses. They are compact, well-controlled, and far less expensive than a linac.

  • Electron beam irradiation from dedicated (often smaller) accelerators has been applied in some inactivation or plant mutagenesis studies, but again, not routinely for viruses.

Heavy-ion beams from specialized accelerators can produce higher mutation frequencies and broader spectra than low-LET radiation like X-rays or electrons, but these are also not linacs and remain specialized tools. For most virus mutation studies, chemical mutagens (e.g., nitrous acid) or error-prone replication systems are even simpler alternatives to any radiation.

In short, while linac-generated radiation could theoretically induce viral mutations, it offers no practical advantage and is far more cumbersome than standard UV, gamma, or X-ray sources.

As support for his hypothesis that SV40 may have been mutated through the use of ionizing radiation, Haslam quoted a book titled Zoonoses of Primates from 1966, which said: "There is, therefore, a serious danger that viruses from such closely related groups as simian primates could show an altered pathogenesis in man, of which malignancy could be a feature. The dangers of such happening are enhanced by man's exposure in crowded cities to oncogenic agents and increased radiation hazards." However the author seems to have said that human exposure to carcinogens (oncogenic agents) and radiation made humans more susceptible to developing cancer, and not that carcinogens or radiation would've induced dangerous mutations in viruses.

  • Wood Irradiation Technology in General: Electron beam (EB) or gamma irradiation for treating wood did exist in the 1950s-1970s era. Researchers explored:

    • Radiation-induced crosslinking or polymerization of monomers impregnated into wood (creating wood-plastic composites).

    • Improving dimensional stability, hardness, or decay resistance.

Could a mattress fire explain Mary Sherman's injuries?

An MD from New Orleans who wrote a review of Haslam's book wrote: "I called Dr. Samuels, who remembers the autopsy and discounts an offsite thermal injury. 'She had severe right-sided burns with exposure of her liver. There was no soot in her lungs meaning that she was dead before any fire. Grok said:

Yes, a smoldering mattress fire (especially one deliberately set with piled clothing and possibly an accelerant) can cause exactly the kind of localized, severe damage described: complete destruction of the right arm and right side of the torso (from waist/hip to shoulder), with soft tissues burned away to expose organs like the liver, intestines, and lung.

Forensic pathologists who have reviewed the actual crime-scene photos, autopsy details, and police reports from Mary Sherman's 1964 death confirm this is consistent with ordinary fire damage - not something that required a lab accident, radiation, or impossible temperatures. Here's why it fits the evidence in the precinct report and newspaper accounts you quoted:Smoldering fires in bedding/mattresses burn slowly but persistently. A smoldering innerspring mattress fire can last for hours, stripping away padding and fabric ("denuding" the springs) while producing intense, localized heat right where the body is in contact. The reports explicitly note the fire smoldered long enough to do exactly that and to burn away the flesh of one arm.

  • The "wick effect" (or candle effect) explains the extreme, one-sided destruction. Human body fat melts and becomes fuel; clothing or bedding acts as a wick. This allows a low-intensity, smoldering fire to consume soft tissue and even char bone over time, while leaving other areas (left side, legs, head hair) relatively untouched. The right side was the area in direct, prolonged contact with the burning mattress and piled underclothing.

  • Accelerant + mattress placement accounts for the unevenness and intensity. Experts note the pattern (right arm and torso "disintegrated," organs exposed, but minimal spread to the rest of the room or body) is typical when someone pours an accelerant on one area, lights the mattress or clothing, and places the burning mattress over or under the body. One pathologist stated: the burned mattress "accounts for the severe fire burns to the right side of her body." Another added that acid or lye couldn't produce this - only heat from fire could. [https://www.nola.com/news/crime_police/in-the-death-of-doctor-mary-sherman-strange-myths-pale-next-to-stranger-facts/article_7022729c-95bf-5fc7-b9ab-8000e1336f6a.html]

  • Temperature and time are sufficient. Burning away soft tissue and charring bone doesn't require crematorium-level sustained heat (1,600-2,000°F for hours). In a prolonged, fat-fueled smoldering fire with bedding as fuel, extremities and torso sections can be destroyed in roughly 50-90 minutes, matching the timeline of a smoldering mattress fire that didn't fully engulf the apartment. [https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0379073898000760]

The official cause of death was stab wounds (including a fatal heart wound) with the burns occurring afterward, almost certainly to obscure evidence. The fire was deliberately set post-mortem. Claims that the damage was "impossible" for a mattress fire (popular in some conspiracy accounts involving a particle accelerator) have been directly refuted by multiple experienced forensic pathologists who examined the case materials.

Grok said:

Tumors described as being as large as the mice themselves, developing in just a week or so from inoculation in weanling mice, are not biologically plausible. A typical laboratory mouse weighs only 15-25 grams. Even the most aggressive mouse tumor models do not produce masses approaching the animal's own body size in such a short time.

In reality, fast-growing transplantable tumors may become palpable within days, but reaching a burdensome size that seriously impairs movement usually takes several weeks, not one. Modern ethical standards limit tumor size to roughly 10% of the mouse's body weight, and even in the less-regulated 1960s, tumors equal in size to the mouse would have caused rapid death from mechanical interference, ulceration, or systemic failure long before that point.

While researchers did harvest, weigh, and passage vigorous tumors - sometimes blending material for serial transplantation - this work was performed on tumors far smaller than the animal itself. The described scenario of mice barely able to drag "huge tumors" the size of their own bodies greatly exaggerates what was realistically possible in cancer research, even with the fastest-growing models available at the time.

A tumor growing to match the entire body weight of a roughly 1-ounce (28-gram) mouse in just one week would be extraordinarily unrealistic based on known biology and laboratory data on mouse tumor models. In cancer research, ethical guidelines for lab mice typically limit single tumors to about 1.5-2 cm in largest diameter, which equates to roughly 2-4 grams or less and often around 10% of body weight at most before euthanasia is required. A 28-gram tumor would be massive, comparable to or exceeding the mouse's own mass, and far beyond any observed or permitted growth. Such a burden would likely kill the animal long before reaching that size due to mechanical stress, ulceration, cachexia, or organ failure.

Tumor growth rates are also much slower in practice. Tumors often become palpable at only a few millimeters days to weeks after cell injection, with measurable growth continuing over additional weeks. Even fast-growing models might show detectable volumes within 4-7 days, and volume doubling times are typically on the order of days to a couple of weeks, but not an explosive increase from near-zero to 28 grams in a single week. Exponential growth does occur, yet starting from a tiny number of implanted cells, it takes far longer to reach even gram-scale masses. Studies routinely monitor growth curves over multiple weeks, with experimental endpoints reached well before extreme sizes.

Biological constraints further make this scenario impossible. Tumors require a blood supply through angiogenesis, along with sufficient nutrients and physical space. Extremely rapid growth would quickly outstrip these resources, leading to widespread necrosis, immune responses, or host death. Even in immunocompromised or specially bred mouse strains designed to allow faster tumor growth than in natural settings, nothing approaches this kind of explosive development. Spontaneous or induced tumors in mice also take much longer periods to form and enlarge. In short, while some laboratory tumors grow relatively fast compared to human ones and may become visible within 1-4 weeks, a full-ounce tumor in one week on a one-ounce mouse belongs firmly in science-fiction territory rather than real oncology.

SV40 doesn't replicate in mice

Judyth Vary Baker claimed that her team created the turbo SV40 through serial passage in mice, where they injected mice with the virus, selected the mice with the largest tumors, and injected the virus from the tumors to subsequent mice.

However SV40 doesn't replicate in mice, so it wouldn't have been possible for the virus to evolve over time to a more cancer-causing form. At best it can induce cancer without replicating to produce a new generation.

Grok said:

No, SV40 (Simian Virus 40) does not replicate productively in mice or mouse cells.

SV40 is a polyomavirus that is permissive (supports full productive replication, including new virion production) in certain simian (monkey) cells, its natural host. It is generally non-permissive in rodent cells, including those from mice.

Key Details:

  • No productive replication: Multiple studies confirm that SV40 does not replicate its DNA or produce infectious progeny virus in mouse cells. Mouse cell extracts do not support SV40 origin-dependent DNA replication in vitro, unlike extracts from permissive human or monkey cells.

  • Transformation instead of replication: In non-permissive rodent cells (mice, hamsters, etc.), SV40 can enter cells, express early genes (like large T antigen), integrate its DNA into the host genome (or persist episomally to some extent), and cause cellular transformation or tumors. However, it does not complete the full lytic cycle.

  • In vivo evidence: When injected into mice, SV40 DNA can persist long-term (mostly in the cytoplasm, with some integration), and under certain conditions (e.g., specific mouse strains or immunosuppression), it can induce tumors. But there is no evidence of viral replication or spread as infectious particles.

This non-permissive behavior in rodents is why SV40 has been a classic model for studying viral oncogenesis and cell transformation without the complications of full viral replication. Transgenic mice expressing SV40 early genes are commonly used to model specific cancers precisely because the virus itself doesn't replicate in them.

Grok also said: "Serial passage relies on repeated cycles of infection → replication → production of progeny virus → harvest and re-infection of new hosts. Without productive replication, there is no meaningful viral evolution, adaptation, or selection for 'more cancer-causing' variants through passage in mice."

If the virus wouldn't have been able to produce more copies, it would've also become diluted over time, because when her team injected a mouse with SV40, not all of the SV40 would've ended up in the tumor where her team supposedly extracted the virus to inject the next batch of mice.

By 1961 standards, Judyth Vary's project on the RPMI HA #5 melanoma strain was not groundbreaking research. It represented an ambitious but fairly routine type of exploratory student work common in cancer research labs at the time.

Mammalian cell culture and media optimization were already well-established techniques by the late 1950s.
