Background

In November 2023, Steve Kirsch published record-level vaccination data from New Zealand which he said he received from a whistleblower who worked for the New Zealand Ministry of Health. [https://kirschsubstack.com/p/data-from-us-medicare-and-the-new] An interview of the whistleblower was published by Liz Gunn, who called the release of the data the "Mother of All Revelations" and who referred to the whistleblower using the pseudonym Winston Smith. [https://rumble.com/v3ynskd-operation-m.o.a.r-mother-of-all-revelations.html]

The real name of the whistleblower is Barry Young. At first his LinkedIn profile said that he has worked at Bank of New Zealand from 2010 until the present, and that he only worked at the NZ Ministry of Health from 2008 to 2010, but later a new entry was added to his profile which said that started to work at the MoH again in 2018: [https://www.linkedin.com/in/barry-young-41a65616/, https://nzougwlgotnday2016.sched.com/barry_young]

Before the new entry at LinkedIn had been added, someone also tweeted: "This person is Barry Young (Winston Smith) who on his LinkedIn Profile is an Oracle DBA at BNZ. At the blue place ex-colleagues have said he left BNZ awhile back to work for MoH." [https://twitter.com/imfeelinwhoopee/status/1730423690624635369]

An article about the whistleblower said: "Te Whatu Ora Health New Zealand is investigating a staff member accused of spreading Covid-19 misinformation using its data." [https://www.rnz.co.nz/news/national/503703/health-nz-staff-member-investigated-for-covid-19-misinformation] Wikipedia says: "Te Whatu Ora is responsible for the planning and commissioning of health services as well as the functions of the 20 former district health boards. The Ministry of Health remains responsible for setting health policy, strategy and regulation." [https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Te_Whatu_Ora] "Te Whatu Ora" is the Maaori name of Health New Zealand, and the Ministry of Health is "Manatū Hauora". Barry Young's LinkedIn profile says that he works for the MoH but I don't know if it's simultaneously possible for him to be employed by Te Whatu Ora. However Wikipedia also says that Te Whatu Ora was only launched in 2022 and it assumed some of the previous functions of the MoH.

Kirsch posted the MOAR data on a cloud storage service called Wasabi, but his Wasabi account got deleted. In an interview with New Zealand Herald, the chief executive of Te Whatu Ora said that "an injunction had been used to have information taken down from an overseas website and cyber security specialists are continuing to scan extensively for any other places where the information may appear". [https://www.nzherald.co.nz/nz/te-whatu-ora-granted-urgent-injunction-police-complaint-filed-over-mass-privacy-breach-of-covid-19-vaccination-data-by-former-staff-member/EUKC3NHC6FAOVL2YJSE4GUR6BU/]

The data is incomplete and obfuscated

Kirsch's Substack post said that "we were only given 4M of the 12M records in New Zealand" and that "The data from New Zealand is not perfect; it is not a complete sample. For example, for some people, the first record in the database is Dose #3." Kirsch said on Twitter that the data he published was "a complete record of all the 'Pay per dose' records in New Zealand". [https://twitter.com/stkirsch/status/1731195035272589748] A post about the whistleblower's interview said: "In the video, Smith explains he was a New Zealand government Ministry of Health employee tasked with building and implementing a digital covid vaccine 'pay-per-dose' system for health care providers. So, every time someone got vaccinated the provider would get compensated." [https://vicparkpetition.substack.com/p/new-zealand-government-whistle-blower]

Kirsch's Wasabi server had a file called About the New Zealand files.docx which provided a bit more details:

The record level .csv file has 4M rows. All the information is randomized so that the statistics are intact but all the fields of every record have been randomized so it will not match any fields of the original record. If there is a match to someone living, that is simply an unavoidable coincidence.

4,193,438 total database records of people who were vaccinated (dead and alive).

2,215,730 unique people are covered in the database.

37,285 unique people died were reported in the data and summarized in the time-series cohort analysis.

66,005 total records for those who have died (so average of less than 2 vaccination records per dead person)

The data is approximately 33% of all New Zealand vaccination records.

Only people who were vaccinated are included in the data. So you cannot use the unvaccinated as a control group.

There was a disproportionate draw on each dose (i.e., for some doses we got a greater percentage of records than other doses).

This database will not contain all records of every person who has a record, e.g., the first record to appear may be on dose 3.

Unvaccinated people never died because the database only had entries for people who received at least one vaccine.

The database is skewed over time in terms of which reports got into this database. That's why you want to look at the death over time for a given dose and deaths per person year, and do NOT compare absolute death rates in a dose unless you are doing a time series cohort analysis where you are calculating death per person days.

I don't understand how it's possible to randomize all fields while simultaneously keeping the statistics intact, because you would have to sacrifice some fidelity in the statistics even if you only randomized a single field. For example is it possible to find the actual number of people who were born on a specific date and who died on a specific date? Or did Kirsch obfuscate the dates of birth but not the years of birth? Or is it possible to find the actual number of people who died on a given day and who had been given a Novavax vaccine? Or are the columns for the date of death or vaccine type obfuscated?

When Kirsch did a presentation about the data at MIT, he said: "You get the original data - which we have obfuscated so we don't get into trouble - it's all HIPAA-compliant - but we preserved all of the fidelity of the data so that we have shifted things such that the statistics are identical even though no record matches anything about any of the people given." [https://rumble.com/v3yovx4-vsrf-live-104-exclusive-mit-speech-by-steve-kirsch.html, time 52:00] But how can the statistics be identical to the real data if "no record matches anything about any of the people given"?

During an interview on InfoWars, Kirsch said: "We have the original data. It's been anonymized so we don't run into any privacy issues or HIPAA violations. But it's been anonymized in a way that we maintain the statistical fidelity of the data. In other words we time-shifted all of the dates relative to each other, but the dates relative to each other are the same. We just shifted them slightly in time so that you can still do the statistical analysis without violating anyone's privacy." [https://banned.video/watch?id=656a5c4e0681e68064e50415, time 14:25] I don't know if Kirsch means that they just shifted all dates backwards or forwards by a constant amount, but that doesn't sound like "randomization", and it might be fairly easy to decode.

The MOAR dataset includes data for about two and half years, because the earliest death is on 2021-05-09 and the last death is on 2023-10-27, and the earliest vaccination is on 2021-04-18 and the last vaccination is on 2023-09-24.

Heatmap for statistics per batch

In batch 1 which has by far the highest percentage of deaths per dose, the average age at death is 86 when rounded to the nearest integer, and the average age at vaccination is 66 for all people and 85 for dead people. The average number of days between death and vaccination is about 403. The average age at vaccination among dead people is below 85 for all batches with an ID between 2 and 93, but in batch 94 and some newer batches the average age at vaccination is 85 or above among dead people. But compared to batch 1, the newer batches were given much more recently, so they have a smaller percentage of deaths per dose because there's not as many people who have died of old age since vaccination:

In the heatmap above, I omitted 4 small batches that had zero deaths. The end of data refers to October 27th 2023 which is the date of the last death included in the dataset. I calculated the person-years by calculating the time from vaccine dose until either death or October 27th 2023, so that I included the time after a patient got a subsequent dose as part of the person-years of earlier doses.

R code:

t=read.csv("nz-record-level-data-4M-records.csv")
t2=t[t$date_of_death!="",]
t1=t[t$batch_id%in%t2$batch_id,]

doses=table(t1$batch_id)
deaths=table(t2$batch_id)
vax1=as.Date(t1$date_time_of_service,"%m-%d-%Y")
death1=as.Date(t1$date_of_death,"%m-%d-%Y")
birth1=as.Date(t1$date_of_birth,"%m-%d-%Y")
vax2=as.Date(t2$date_time_of_service,"%m-%d-%Y")
death2=as.Date(t2$date_of_death,"%m-%d-%Y")
birth2=as.Date(t2$date_of_birth,"%m-%d-%Y")
dend=max(death2)

d=data.frame(`Doses given`=c(doses),check.names=F)
d$`Deaths`=deaths
d$`Percentage of deaths per dose`=100*deaths/doses
d$`Percentage of deaths per person-year`=deaths/(tapply(pmin(death1,dend,na.rm=T)-vax1,t1$batch_id,sum)/365.2422)*1e2
d$`Average days between vaccination and death`=tapply(death2-vax2,t2$batch_id,mean)
d$`Average age at vaccination`=tapply(vax1-birth1,t1$batch_id,mean)/365.2422
d$`Average age at vaccination for dead recipients`=tapply(vax2-birth2,t2$batch_id,mean)/365.2422
d$`Average days from dose given to end of data`=tapply(dend-vax2,t2$batch_id,mean)
d$`Average days from death to end of data`=tapply(dend-death2,t2$batch_id,mean)

m=t(apply(d,2,\(x)(x-min(x))/(max(x)-min(x))))

kimir2=\(x){x[]=ifelse(or(x>=1e6,x<=-1e6),paste0(round(x/1e6,ifelse(abs(x)<1e7,1,0)),"M"),ifelse(or(x>=1e3,x<=-1e3),paste0(round(x/1e3,ifelse(abs(x)<1e4,1,0)),"k"),round(x)));x}
disp=kimir2(t(d))
disp[3,]=sprintf("%.1f",d[,3])
disp[4,]=sprintf("%.1f",d[,4])

slices=3
for(i in 1:slices){
  step=ceiling(ncol(m)/slices)
  start=(i-1)*step+1
  end=min(start+step-1,ncol(m))
  pheatmap::pheatmap(
    m[,start:end],
    filename=paste0("i",i,".png"),
    cluster_rows=F,
    cluster_cols=F,
    legend=F,
    cellwidth=20,
    cellheight=20,
    fontsize=9,
    border_color=NA,
    display_numbers=disp[,start:end],
    fontsize_number=8,

    breaks=seq(0,1,,256),
    colorRampPalette(hex(HSV(c(210,210,130,60,40,20,0),c(0,.5,.5,.5,.5,.5,.5),1)))(256)
  )
}

Barry Young's method of counting days with 120 or more deaths

In his presentation Young pointed out that in the 2010s, New Zealand had only a handful of days which had 120 or more deaths, such as during the Christchurch earthquake in 2011, but in 2021 and 2022 after COVID vaccines had been rolled out, there was a much higher number of days which had 120 or more deaths:

However according to the Short-Term Mortality Fluctuations dataset, the average number of deaths per day in New Zealand increased from about 83 in 2011 to about 94 in 2019:

> t=read.csv("https://www.mortality.org/File/GetDocument/Public/STMF/Outputs/NZL_NPstmfout.csv")
> t2=t[t$Sex=="b"&t$Year>=2011,]
> round(tapply(t2$Total,t2$Year,mean)/7,1)

 82.9  82.3  80.7  85.0  86.8  85.6  92.0  90.8  93.6  89.5  95.6 105.7 103.3

So therefore it would make more sense to use the linear trend in deaths before COVID as the baseline, and to then count how many days have a number of deaths that's more than a given threshold above the baseline. [https://twitter.com/UncleJo46902375/status/1730241561424732620] Below I calculated a linear trend for the data from 2011-2019, and I counted how many weeks each year had where the number of deaths was 2 or more standard deviations above the average, where I got the standard deviation from the weekly difference to the trend in 2011-2019. But the number of weeks above the threshold was only 1 in 2021, because there were almost no COVID deaths in 2021:

For some reason there's 4 people who were posthumously vaccinated so that the date of death is before the date of vaccination. The most common durations from vaccination to death are 100 and 170 days on shared first place:

If the date of death in the CSV file is the date of registration and not the date of occurrence, and if most deaths have a registration delay of at least one day, then it would explain why there are only 7 deaths which occured on the same day as the vaccination. And it would also explain why there's a low number of deaths during the first week after vaccination. As far as I know, Kirsch has not specified if the date of death was the date when the death occurred or the date when the death was registered.

In 2021-2023, the average number of deaths per day in New Zealand would be about 95-100 based on the linear trend from 2011-2019, and even if about 67% of the records would be missing, (95-100)/2*.33 would still be about 32. Even though I guess if people got vaccinated during the working day, then the average time of day for vaccination might be after midday, and people who died earlier in the day can't have died before the vaccination. But there's also a low number of deaths for 1 day and 2 days after vaccination.

The MOAR dataset is missing all vaccine doses given in the first three months of 2021, so the earliest date of vaccination in the dataset is 2021-04-18, and the number of missing vaccination doses is disproportionately high in the first half of 2021. The latest date of death is on 2023-10-27. So in the histogram above, the number of deaths tapers off at the end of the x-axis and there is only a small number of deaths that occurred more than 800 days from a vaccination, but that's because the dataset only includes a small number of people who were vaccinated early enough that it was possible for them to die more than 800 days from the vaccination.

The Israeli report only looked at second vaccine doses, and it only included deaths from January to October 2021: "All those vaccinated in two doses until the end of October 2021 (before the start of the omicron wave) who died during the 60-day follow-up from the vaccination and who were not positive for corona (1815 cases) were included." So in order to die exactly 200 days after the second dose, a subject of the study would've needed to have received the second dose on April 14th or earlier. And if a subject of the study got the second dose on October 1st, then they couldn't have died more than 30 days after the second dose.

Distribution of vaccine doses given per day compared to OWID

The proportion of vaccine doses that are missing from the MOAR dataset seems to get lower over time, so that it is high in early 2021 but much lower in late 2022 and 2023:

The MOAR data looks like it might be a smoothed average, because there are no clear dips in the number of vaccines given on weekends like at OWID. And at OWID there's a big spike in the number of vaccine doses given on October 16th 2021, but a similar spike is not visible in the MOAR data.

If you divide the average number of doses given on weekdays with the average number of doses given on weekends, the ratio is about 1.53 at OWID but about 0.96 in the MOAR dataset:

The MOAR data is supposed to consist of vaccine records for "pay-per-dose" vaccines, so the pay-per-dose vaccines might have a different weekday-weekend ratio than other vaccines. Actually in my plot above for the MOAR data, there are periods when there is regularly a slightly higher number of vaccine doses given on weekends, which is the easiest to see around July-August 2022 and April-June 2023.
