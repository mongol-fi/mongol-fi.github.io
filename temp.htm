Did Himmler send Traub on a trip to Turkey?

In his book Finnegan wrote: "In Dr. Kurt Blöme's talks with Heinrich Himmler, commander of the SS-Waffen (Schutzstaffel) regarding biological warfare, it was decided to secure strains of rinderpest, but securing it was not so easy. The obstacle was due to established laws across Europe forbidding them from storing the virus due to its highly contagious nature that could wipe out Europe's livestock, which gave them great difficulty in finding strains, and eventually, Himmler decided to send Traub on a trip to Turkey to secure cultures of rinderpest and he made the long journey there and back.[72] Nearing the end of the war, Himmler, had been pushing for more aggressive biological warfare development with his medical and agricultural authorities, Kurt Blöme, Walter Schrieber, and Otto Waldman." NARS."

When I asked Grok what other documents support the claim that Traub took a trip to Turkey, Grok answered:

The account of Erich Traub's trip to Turkey to secure rinderpest virus strains for Nazi biological warfare efforts originates almost exclusively from a single postwar interrogation of Dr. Kurt Blome conducted by scientists from the ALSOS mission. This interview is preserved in the Joint Intelligence Objectives Agency administrative records held at the National Archives, specifically under RG 330 INSCOM dossier XE001248. Blome described how, amid strict European laws prohibiting the storage of the highly contagious rinderpest virus due to its potential to devastate livestock across the continent, Himmler directed the search for viable strains. When initial efforts faltered, Traub was dispatched on the journey to Turkey, where he reportedly obtained cultures before returning to Germany. No separate contemporaneous German documents, such as travel authorizations, laboratory logs from the Riems Island facility, shipping manifests for the virus samples, or independent reports from other participants, have been identified in declassified archives to stand alone as confirmation of the mission.

Secondary historical literature that references the episode consistently traces its details back to this same Blome statement without introducing fresh primary evidence. Michael Christopher Carroll's 2004 book Lab 257 dramatizes the journey, portraying Traub's personal search along the Black Sea coast of Turkey amid Anatolian terrain in pursuit of a virulent strain intended for use against the Allies, yet it relies directly on the ALSOS-derived intelligence and adds no new archival citations or independent verification. The narrative in such works often highlights the failure of the acquired strain, which proved nonvirulent upon testing at Riems, leading to the shelving of rinderpest weaponization plans in favor of continued focus on foot-and-mouth disease.

Amanda Kay McVety's 2018 scholarly study The Rinderpest Campaigns similarly incorporates the Turkey trip as part of the broader context of wartime biological research efforts, noting Blome's limited recollection of the exact acquisition details while accepting the mission's occurrence under Himmler's push for aggressive development involving figures like Walter Schreiber and Otto Waldmann. However, McVety's analysis draws from the same postwar intelligence files rather than unearthing distinct sources such as Turkish veterinary records, border crossing documentation, or Allied intelligence summaries from the period that might corroborate Traub's movements independently.

Additional works examining Operation Paperclip and Nazi scientist dossiers, including Linda Hunt's Secret Agenda and various accounts referencing RG 330 materials, mention the episode only within the framework of Blome's interrogation and Traub's role as lab chief at Riems under the chain of command that included Himmler, Leonardo Conti, and Blome. These texts offer interpretive context on the biological warfare program but do not present supplementary paperwork, such as visa records, expense reports, or Soviet-zone postwar intelligence that could verify the journey on its own merits. Wikipedia entries and derivative online summaries uniformly echo the same limited sourcing, often citing Carroll's Lab 257 or the underlying National Archives file.

In summary, while the story of Traub's long journey to and from Turkey has been repeated across popular and academic histories of Nazi biological weapons research and its postwar legacies, the supporting documentation remains narrowly confined to that one postwar ALSOS interview of Kurt Blome. Later books and articles function primarily as layers of interpretation and dramatization rather than providers of independent corroborative records from the wartime era itself.
