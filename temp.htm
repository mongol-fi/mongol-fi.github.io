The chromosome of Borrelia anserina undergoes extremely limited sequence alteration from laboratory modifications such as serial egg passages and experimental attempts to adapt the spirochete to novel hosts like mice, lizards, turtles, frogs, fish, or pigeons. These procedures, as pursued in the 1930s by researchers including Franz Jahnel and later maintained through egg passages under Erich Traub and Werner Schäfer, rely on selective pressure in vivo or in ovo to favor variants better suited to new environments or to shift tissue tropisms. However, the linear chromosome of approximately 905 kilobases encodes essential housekeeping genes with high synteny across isolates, rendering it resistant to wholesale remodeling. Instead, adaptation typically involves minor point mutations, small indels, or plasmid-level variations that fine-tune surface proteins or virulence without disrupting core genomic architecture. Whole-genome comparisons of sequenced B. anserina strains confirm this stability, showing that even strains maintained under differing laboratory conditions diverge by only a handful of nucleotide positions. [https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5356049/]

Alignment of the chromosome from strain Es (906,833 base pairs) with strain BA2 (904,790 base pairs) identifies just four transversions and 37 single-nucleotide indels across the shared sequence. This results in roughly 0.005 percent overall divergence, a level so low that it represents negligible change for practical purposes. Comparable conservation appears across Borrelia species, where chromosomal gene content and order remain nearly identical despite ecological specialization, underscoring that host-switching experiments do not drive rapid or extensive chromosomal evolution. In related attenuation studies using serial passages in liquid medium rather than eggs, loss of chick infectivity by the twelfth passage correlated with shifts in protein profiles detectable by electrophoresis, yet electron microscopy revealed no structural genomic differences and no evidence of broad chromosomal mutation. [https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/2300719/]

These findings indicate that the pre-World War II efforts to remodel Borrelia anserina for neurotropic or mammalian models produced at most trace chromosomal tweaks through natural selection during limited passages, far below thresholds that would redefine the organism's genetic identity. Plasmids, which carry many variable surface antigens, absorb most adaptive burden, leaving the chromosome essentially intact at greater than 99.99 percent identity to progenitor strains. Consequently, modifications of the type described would alter the B. anserina chromosome by approximately 0.005 percent, preserving its fundamental sequence and synteny while permitting phenotypic shifts sufficient for experimental utility. This genomic resilience explains why avian spirochetosis agents have retained their core identity despite decades of laboratory manipulation.

Adaptation to a new species through serial passage does not necessarily increase the rate of mutation by several orders of magnitude.
