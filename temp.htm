Amyloidogenic or aggregation-prone sequences (APRs) are very common across proteins. These short segments, typically 5-15 residues long and often hydrophobic with high β-sheet propensity, occur frequently in polypeptide chains. Predictive tools such as TANGO, WALTZ, and AGGRESCAN, along with large-scale proteome analyses, indicate that APRs appear on average at least once every ~50-100 amino acids in typical protein sequences. In globular proteins, roughly 15-20% of residues fall within such predicted aggregation-prone regions, though these are usually buried in the hydrophobic core or stabilized by native folding to prevent uncontrolled aggregation.

Proteome-wide surveys confirm this high prevalence. In a comprehensive analysis of plant proteomes spanning 75 species and more than 2.9 million protein sequences using the WALTZ algorithm, more than half of all proteins in each proteome contained at least one predicted amyloidogenic region, with a median of around 25% of proteins after applying stricter filters. [https://www.mdpi.com/1422-0067/18/10/2155] Similar patterns hold in other organisms, including bacteria and eukaryotes. Many proteins - perhaps most - harbor the potential to form amyloid-like structures under permissive conditions such as fragmentation, denaturation, high local concentration, or specific environmental stresses.

In intrinsically disordered regions (IDRs), cryptic amyloidogenic regions (CARs) of milder, often more polar propensity are also abundant. Studies using adjusted WALTZ thresholds have shown that over 50% (and up to 80% depending on stringency) of IDRs contain at least one such CAR, with an average density of roughly 2-3 CARs per 100 residues in many datasets. [https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8349759/] These regions frequently overlap with functional interaction motifs and .g//pggpnre thought to contribute to normal biology rather than solely posing a risk of pathology.

Evolutionarily, amyloidogenic motifs are often over-represented relative to randomized sequences of the same amino acid composition, suggesting they can serve useful functional roles in protein interactions, structural stabilization, or regulated aggregation, while being actively suppressed by mechanisms such as molecular chaperones, gatekeeper residues (e.g., prolines or charged amino acids), and proper three-dimensional folding. Exact frequencies vary with the prediction algorithm, chosen score thresholds, sequence length cutoffs, and protein class (globular versus disordered), but the overall picture is consistent: sequences similar to those identified in the SARS-CoV-2 spike protein represent a routine and widespread feature of the protein universe rather than an unusual anomaly. Pathological aggregation typically requires additional triggers such as proteolysis, mutations, inflammation, or other stressors that expose or liberate these otherwise controlled segments.
