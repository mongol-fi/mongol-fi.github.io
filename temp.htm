Palts's activities are under the spotlight of the kapo in connection with a criminal case in which the managers of a subsidiary of the law firm Heta are suspected of large-scale tax fraud. "The Security Police is conducting a criminal case concerning the activities of the managers of Heta Inc., registered in a tax-free zone, in connection with a possible tax crime," commented kapo Commissioner Olari Valtin.

The motive for initiating criminal proceedings was a possible violation of the Taxation Act and the Tax Act, which resulted in a million kroons or more not being collected in taxes. The penalty under this section is imprisonment for a term of three to seven years.

The criminal case was initiated by the State Prosecutor's Office on May 5 this year based on materials received from the Tax Board. On the same day, the prosecutor's office sent the case to the Security Police Board for further investigation.

Kapo did not give any further comments on the matter. It is known that various investigative measures, including seizures, have been taken during the criminal proceedings. For example, the day before yesterday, documents were seized at the offices of Tele2, formerly Levicom, Tõnis Palts' home and office address at Kiriku põik 4 in Tallinn, and at the accounting firm Loomani Konsultatsioonid. So far, no one has been identified as a suspect or accused in the criminal case.

"Incredibly intelligent and likeable young men," said Reet Umjärv, an accountant at Levicom Finance, a company associated with Palts, describing the taking of documents. "We exchanged papers, one asked, the other gave, in an atmosphere of mutual understanding."

According to Umjärv, the security police officers' questions concerned the businesses of the so-called P-boys, or Paltsu, Peek and Pedriks (Paltsu's business partners Toomas Peek and Markus Pedriks? ed.). "They came and knew exactly what to ask," Umjärv stated.

Tõnis Palts justified his resignation as Minister of Finance last week, among other things, by intensifying attacks on him. When announcing his resignation on Friday, it became public that the Tax Board had five tax audits and one data audit pending against Palts and his businesses. The connection of Palts' businesses to the criminal case was not made public at the time.

Prime Minister Juhan Parts did not confirm yesterday that the outgoing minister was involved in a criminal case as a motive for Palts' departure. Parts said through the head of the government's press office Kristi Liiva that he knew nothing about the criminal case that had been initiated and that Palts' resignation was his own wish. Palts' advisor Anneli Entson also said yesterday that she was not aware of the criminal case in question.

Palts himself was in Dubai for the International Monetary Fund's annual meeting and could not be reached yesterday. However, Ants Karu, the lawyer representing Tõnis Palts, refused to comment on the raids on companies related to Palts without consulting his client.

Palts' business partner Toomas Peek said that indeed, the day before yesterday, the Security Police went to question people at the Tele2 office and took away some original documents. According to Peek, he had the impression that this was the same matter that the Tax Board began investigating in February this year. The problem is the consulting contracts of Levicom companies from 1997-1998. Heta Inc, a subsidiary of one of Estonia's best-known law firms, Heta, is also involved in these contracts.

Üllar Talviste, one of the owners of Heta and the chairman of the bar association related to the activities of Heta Inc., said yesterday that he was aware of the raids at Tele2 and Kiriku põik 4. However, he was not willing to specify in what connection the raids were made. Talviste said that he has a negative attitude towards the fact that the capo is associating him with organizing tax fraud.
