Number of pre-2020 BLAST matches to segments surrounding Pradhan's inserts

Pradhan et al. did a pairwise alignment of SARS-CoV-2 against the original SARS-CoV, and they did BLAST searches for the segmetns of SARS-CoV-2 where there were gaps in SARS-CoV, along with an unspecified number of residues surrounding the gaps. The authors didn't specify how many surrounding residues around the gaps they searched for.

But the first match TNGTKR contains 0 residues outside the segment with gaps in SARS-CoV. The second match HKNNKS contains 4 residues outside the segment with gaps. The third match RSYL----TPGDSSSG contains 6 residues outside the segment with gaps. In the fourth match QTNSPRRA, the first 2 and last 2 residues are outside the segment with gaps (because the gaps in the alignment were inserted at NSPR and not PRRA). So the maximum number of surrounding residues in any of the matches is 6.

This table shows each of Pradhan's inserts and 6 surrounding residues on either side:

Number Leading 6 Insert Trailing 6
1 HAIHVS GTNGTKR FDNPVL
2 DPFLGV YYHK NNKSWM

4 SYQTQT NSPR RARSVA

I went to protein BLAST: https://blast.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/Blast.cgi?PROGRAM=blastp&PAGE_TYPE=BlastSearch. I switched the database from "nr_cluster_seq" to "nr", which used to be the default database in 2020 when Pradhan et al. wrote their paper. I selected the species as viruses, and I excluded the species "Betacoronavirus pandemicum (taxid:3418604)" (which is the new species name for SARS-CoV-2 and other sarbecoviruses). Protein BLAST doesn't support Entrez queries, so I wasn't able to use a query like 0:2019[dp] to filter out sequences published since 2020. Then I searched for each of the 4 inserts in the table above along with the 6 surrounding residues on either side. The vast majority of the matches were to viruses other than HIV. In order to retrieve the number of matches to each query by species, I clicked "Download" and selected "Hit Table (text)". Then I retrieved the XML metadata for each sequence and selected the species name from the metadata:

The reason why Pradhan focused on the matches to HIV was that all 4 inserts happened to have matches to HIV, and not because the matches to HIV were particularly close. But if their BLAST database had hundreds of thousands of protein sequences of HIV but only dozens of protein sequences of some obscure virus, then of course.

I tried searching for the first 7-aa insert in protein BLAST: https://blast.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/Blast.cgi?PROGRAM=blastp&PAGE_TYPE=BlastSearch. I entered GTNGTKR to the text field at the top, I set the database to refseq_protein, I set the organism as "Viruses (taxid:10239)", and I clicked BLAST.

There were many different bacteriopages and a few non-phage viruses which matched 6 out of 7 aa so that one aa at either end was clipped by the local alignment, so that there was 100% identity but 85% query coverage:

HIV-1 was not included in the results above, because I searched in a database which only contained refseq proteins. The standard reference genome of HIV-1 is called HXB2, and its envelope protein matches only 3 out of 7 aa of Pradhan's first insert:

Next I tried doing another BLAST search for Pradhan's first insert, but I set the "Non-redundant protein sequences (nr)" database and I set the organism to HIV-1. There were 3 different spots of the envelope protein which all matched 6/7 aa of the insert so that one aa at either end was cut off:

There's more than a million sequences of HIV-1 in GenBank: https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/nuccore/?term=%22Human+immunodeficiency+virus+1%22%5Bporgn%3A%5f%5ftxid11676%5D. I don't know how many of them are included at BLAST, but in any case it's not that unusual that a handful of sequences would randomly happen to match 6 out of 7 aa of Pradhan's first insert.

I used this query at GenBank to search for full envelope protein sequences of HIV-1 published before 2020: (env OR envelope) 0:2019[dp] 2000:3000[slen] "Human immunodeficiency virus 1"[porgn:__txid11676]. [https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/nuccore/?term=%28env+OR+envelope%29+0%3A2019%5Bdp%5D+2000%3A3000%5Bslen%5D+%22Human+immunodeficiency+virus+1%22%5Bporgn%3A__txid11676%5D] I then downloaded the results by clicking "Send to:", clicking "File", and changing "Format" to "FASTA".

There were a total of 57,625 results.

I downloaded an alignment of 10,733 HIV-1 envelope protein sequences by going here, changing "DNA/Protein" to "Protein", clicking "Get Alignment", and clicking "Download this alignment": https://www.hiv.lanl.gov/content/sequence/NEWALIGN/align.html.

The set of sequences included a total of 14,183 perfect matches to a set of 100,000 random 6 aa sequences:

$ seqkit seq -g HIV1_ALL_2022_env_PRO.fasta|seqkit replace -sp\# -rX>HIV1_ALL_2022_env_PRO.ungap
$ tr -dc ACDEFGHIKLMNPQRSTVWY</dev/random|head -c$[6*100000]|sed 's/....../&\n/g'|awk '{print">"NR}1'>random10k.fa
$ seqkit locate -Pf random10k.fa HIV1_ALL_2022_env_PRO.ungap>matches100k
$ sed 1d matches100k|wc -l
14183

The matches occurred in 1,013 unique segments out of the 10,000 random 6 aa segments:

$ sed 1d matches100k|cut -f7|sort -u|wc -l
1013

Then I kept one random match for each segment, and I converted the starting position of each match to HXB2 coordinates:

sed 1d matches100k|gshuf|awk '!a[$7]++'|cut -f1,5|while read l m;do seqkit grep -p$l<0/HIV1_ALL_2022_env_PRO.fasta|seqkit seq -s|awk -F '' '{for(i=1;i<=NF;i++){gaps+=$i=="-";if(i-gaps==x){print i;exit}}}' x=$m;done>alnpos

seqkit head -n1 HIV1_ALL_2022_env_PRO.fasta|seqkit seq -s|cat - alnpos|awk -F '' 'NR==1{for(i=1;i<=NF;i++){pos+=$i!="-";a[i]=pos};next}{print a[$0]}'>hxbpos
