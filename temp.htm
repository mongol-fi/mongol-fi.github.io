Anserina as an outgroup in a rooted tree

Finnegan wrote: "Richard Marconi had in fact put forward in his paper that the Lyme Borreliosis clade descended from Borrelia anserina, which served as an outgroup". In the paper by Marconi et al., the authors made a phylogenetic tree where they picked anserina as the outgroup, but that doesn't mean the authors suggested the other species were descended from anserina: [https://journals.asm.org/doi/pdf/10.1128/jcm.33.9.2427-2434.1995]

If you want to turn an unrooted tree to a rooted tree, you have to pick some node as an outgroup. Then the earliest branch in the tree will be formed between the outgroup and other nodes. It's typical for the node that is the most distant from the main cluster of nodes to be selected as the outgroup, like how in Marconi's tree there were 11 nodes from the LB group and 1 node from the RF group, so the node from the RF group was selected as the outgroup. If the authors would've made a tree with 11 nodes from the RF group and one node from the LB group, the authors could've similarly selected the node from the LB group as the outgroup, but that doesn't mean that the authors would've suggested that the RF group was descended from the LB group.

What it actually demonstrated was a short-term, artificial laboratory trick: using UV-inactivated Sendai virus to force the fusion of T. parva-infected bovine lymphoid cells with uninfected baby hamster kidney (BHK) cells (or mouse cells in related work). This created transient heterokaryons - hybrid cells with mixed bovine and hamster nuclei sharing a common cytoplasm. In these fused cells, the intracellular parasites could be detected for a limited time, sometimes appearing in regions that looked like they contained mostly hamster nuclei.

This was a proof-of-concept for moving a strictly cell-associated parasite across a species barrier in vitro under highly artificial conditions. It did not result in:

  • Stable, continuous propagation of T. parva in hamster (or mouse) cell lines without the bovine component.

  • Genetic adaptation or evolutionary change in the parasite itself that allowed it to infect or replicate independently in a new host species.

  • Successful infection of live hamsters or any whole-animal model with a viable, transmissible T. parva strain.

Switching vectors, hosts, tropisms, and disease patterns at that scale in a few years - even with aggressive serial passaging, co-infections, or "Russian-doll" cocktails - would be unprecedented in bacterial evolution. Anserina as an outgroup in a rooted tree

In the paper by Marconi et al., the authors made a phylogenetic tree where they picked anserina as the outgroup, but that doesn't mean the authors suggested the other species were descended from anserina: [https://journals.asm.org/doi/pdf/10.1128/jcm.33.9.2427-2434.1995]

(The double slash symbol in the branch for anserina means that the true length of the branch is not displayed, because it would've taken about three times as much space as the whole width of the figure.)

If you want to turn an unrooted tree to a rooted tree, you have to pick some node as an outgroup. Then the earliest branch in the tree will be formed between the outgroup and other nodes. It's typical for the node that is the most distant from the main cluster of nodes to be selected as the outgroup, like how in Marconi's tree there were 11 nodes from the LB group and 1 node from the RF group, so the node from the RF group was selected as the outgroup. If the authors would've made a tree with 11 nodes from the RF group and one node from the LB group, the authors could've similarly selected the node from the LB group as the outgroup, but that doesn't mean that the authors would've suggested that the RF group was descended from the LB group.

Heartwater (caused by Ehrlichia ruminantium) and East Coast fever (caused by Theileria parva) are both diseases that affect cattle and that are associated with severe economic loss in affected regions. Borrelia burgdorferi can infect horses, cattle, sheeps, goats, and pigs, but the infection is often considered asymptomatic or subclinical. The earliest paper I found about a Borrelia burgdorferi infection in livestock was published in 1988. [https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/3190095/]

Another reason why Lyme disease was not studied at Plum Island may be that Borrelia burgdorferi is not recognized as a pathogen with potential to be employed as a bioweapon against livestock.

No, I don't agree. The idea that the entire Lyme borreliosis clade (Borrelia burgdorferi sensu lato, or LB group, including species like B. burgdorferi s.s., B. afzelii, B. garinii, etc.) rapidly derived from Borrelia anserina (an avian relapsing-fever spirochete) in just a few years under lab-driven "biological arms race" conditions is not supported by the scientific evidence on Borrelia phylogeny, genomics, or evolutionary rates. It's a creative hypothesis from the Substack piece (which frames Lyme as engineered starting ~1939 via German labs like Insel Riems, serial egg passages, co-infections, phages/mycoplasma as gene shuttles, and later Plum Island work under Erich Traub), but it requires rejecting core molecular data in favor of circumstantial historical overlaps. Here's why it doesn't hold up.

1. Phylogeny shows deep, ancient divergence - not recent derivation

  • B. anserina is the type species of the genus Borrelia and sits firmly in the relapsing-fever (RF) clade (along with agents like B. hermsii, B. duttonii, etc.). The Lyme borreliosis group forms a separate, monophyletic clade (sometimes reclassified as Borreliella) that is sister to the RF group but clearly distinct.

  • Multiple independent studies (using 16S rRNA, flaB, multi-locus sequence typing/MLST, whole-chromosome SNPs, conserved housekeeping genes, and full genomes) consistently root the LB tree with RF species (including B. anserina or close relatives) as the outgroup. LB species cluster together with their own internal structure; there's no evidence of LB "branching off" from B. anserina itself.

  • Morphological similarities or shared epitopes (e.g., flagellar proteins) noted in older papers are superficial and expected in related but divergent spirochetes - they don't override genomic phylogeny. Horizontal gene transfer (HGT) via phages does occur in Borrelia, but it doesn't rewrite the core chromosomal backbone enough to collapse a deep clade split into a recent event. Modern phylogenomics (hundreds of single-copy orthologs) confirms the split is real and ancient.

2. Divergence times and diversity are incompatible with a 1930s lab origin

  • Genomic dating puts the most recent common ancestor (MRCA) of B. burgdorferi s.s. (the main North American Lyme agent) at roughly ~60,000 years ago (with credible intervals placing it well before the Last Glacial Maximum ~20,000 years ago). Diversity is ancient, geographically structured, and shows pre-human migration patterns across North America - long predating any 20th-century labs.

  • The broader LB clade shows even deeper roots, with species radiations tied to host/vector adaptations over tens of thousands of years (e.g., bird vs. mammal specialists like B. garinii vs. B. bavariensis). This matches natural phylogeography, not a sudden post-1939 "starburst" radiation from one lab strain.

  • Even within the RF group, divergences (e.g., louse-borne B. recurrentis from its tick-borne ancestor) are estimated at ~4,000-8,000 years ago - still orders of magnitude older than "several years." A lab-driven jump from RF (B. anserina) to a whole new LB clade in the 1930s-1940s would require implausibly fast, coordinated changes across the chromosome, plasmids, and regulatory networks while leaving no detectable "lab signature" in modern genomes.

3. Biology and lab realities make the rapid "arms race" transformation implausible

  • B. anserina is adapted to birds, soft ticks (argasid), and primarily causes acute septicemic disease with transovarial transmission. Lyme agents are tissue-tropic, use hard ticks (ixodid), persist chronically in mammals, and have a very different plasmid complement (e.g., OspA/OspC surface proteins, cp26, lp54, etc.). Switching vectors, hosts, tropisms, and disease patterns at that scale in a few years - even with aggressive serial passaging, co-infections, or "Russian-doll" cocktails - would be unprecedented in bacterial evolution. Lab evolution can drive fast adaptation (e.g., antibiotic resistance or host jumps in controlled settings), but not the creation of an entire ecologically distinct clade that perfectly mimics millions of years of natural divergence.

  • The global biowarfare context (Nazi tick research, Paperclip transfers, Plum Island releases) is real and worth studying - ticks and borreliae were indeed investigated as vectors/simulants. But no declassified records, genomic forensics, or epidemiological patterns support B. anserina being the direct precursor to Lyme borreliae. Lyme's emergence in the 1970s aligns better with ecological changes (reforestation, deer/tick population booms, suburban sprawl) acting on a pre-existing natural pathogen.

The Substack piece rightly notes that pre-1939 B. anserina sequences are limited and that phylogeny isn't "absolute" under extreme human influence - but the sheer volume of independent genomic datasets (from wild ticks, ancient samples, global isolates) all converge on the same ancient natural history. Dismissing that as universally skewed by HGT or manipulation would require extraordinary evidence, which isn't presented.
