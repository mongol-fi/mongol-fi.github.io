Traub's research on bacteria

Traub was a virologist, and he did little work with bacteria judging from his bibliography. Claims that he researched Borrelia bacteria go back to sources like unpublished intelligence files or to anecdotal information conveyed by Loftus and Carroll.

Even on Finnegan's Erich Traub timeline, there is little evidence that Traub worked with bacteria: [https://www.thesleeperagent.com/erich-traub]

  • "1937 (March) - Traub supervises researcher John B. Nelson in further looking into the unusual respiratory infections that resulted in the mouse colony after the LCM epidemic and the arrival of a new stock of Swiss mice from the same source, The respiratory infection was a complexity of Mycoplasma and other infections, due to immunocompromise." He linked to three papers about "Nelson's experiments under Traub", but Nelson was the only author listed in the papers, so Traub didn't necessarily have any major contribution to the studies. [https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC2133528/, https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC2133524/, https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC2133521/]

  • "1940 - Traub, Beller, and another colleague carry out experiments on bovine papular stomatitis virus, a virus that closely resembles Foot-and-Mouth Disease (FMD) and Vesicular Stomatitis, but is a poxvirus and a very close relative to vaccinia virus and smallpox. [...] It was in this paper that we can see that he used the virus to weaponize the simulant bacteria Serratia marcescens (then called Bacillus prodigiosus) that would later be used in his American simulant testing activities." In his book Finnegan wrote: "In April of 1940, Traub, Beller, and another colleague published a study of Bovine Papular Stomatitis, a poxvirus similar to smallpox, in 'Studies on Bovine Papular Stomatitis,' a viral infection that looked much like Foot-and-Mouth Disease but is a member of the poxvirus family. They began experiments trying to adapt it to different animals to change its properties.[21] It was in this study, they mixed the virus in broth containing strains of Serratia marcescens (Bacillus prodigiosus), a so-called 'harmless' bacterium to cattle and was used in later simulant tests in America. The filters used hold back the bacteria as the mixture passes through the filter and any virus will pass through it, but the bacteria will be held back. In this case however, the virus had transferred genetic material to the bacteria giving it pathogenic properties and changing its qualities.[22] In other words, he weaponized the simulant bacteria Serratia marcescens.[23]" The untranslated title of the paper is "Untersuchungen uber die stomatitis papulosa des Rindes", but I didn't find the paper posted online. But how was Traub even able to tell that the virus had transferred genetic material to the bacteria, because genetic sequencing had not yet been invented in 1940? Even DNA had not yet been discovered. And it's unlikely that random genetic material from a virus would even be functional in a bacteria, or that the lucky acquisition of a single functional piece of viral DNA would somehow amount to a weaponization of the bacteria. And anyway, the 1940 study was a study about viruses, where bacteria appear to have been only employed as part of bacterial filters (like Chamberland or Berkefeld filters, which were common in virology at the time).

  • "1941 - An outbreak of unknown origin occurs in Army horses owned by a veterinary company, with horses showing signs of broncho-pneumonia. They set up a handful of experiments to assess the cause of disease. They set up experiments on blood parasites, equine infectious anemia, and infectious respiratory catarrh (mixed infections). They conclude the disease was due to infectious respiratory catarrh, after finding Streptococcus equi, Streptococcus pyogenes, and other bacterium that are usually considered 'harmless' to the horse." Traub was the only author in the paper about the study, but he might have been part of a team that also included experts on bacterial or parasitical diseases.

  • "1942 - Traub concludes his lengthy studies of infectious respiratory catarrh with a focus on hemolytic Streptococci, A form of Streptococcus that destroys red blood cells, in: The Presence of Hemolytic Streptococci in the Infectious Respiratory Catarrh Disease of the Horse's Respiratory System (Traub and Gratzl 1942)". This seems like a follow-up to the study in military horses.

  • "1942 - Traub conducts an investigation into a disease of rabbits in Angora for the Luftwaffe, after rabbits in the area started dying of an unknown disease. The disease caused the rabbits' ears to become droopy, their heads and neck would become twisted and disfigured, in some ways resembling amyotrophic lateral sclerosis (ALS) and paralytic polio. He finds an invasive bacterium in the brains of the rabbits, known as Listeria monocytogenes, which is a cause of food poisoning (listeriosis) in humans." This appears to have been a case where Traub had to do field work for the military outside his field of expertise, similar to the study where he had to find the cause of a disease outbreak in military horses. Traub was again the only author in the paper about the rabbit study, but I don't know if he was part of a team that also included experts on bacterial diseases.

  • "1947 - Traub initiates a series of experiments in the immunization of swine erysipelas, (Erysipelothrix rhusiopathiae), a bacterium similar to Brucella that causes severe septicemia and big red rashes in swine. Traub concentrated the antigen which were used for vaccines with a unique formulation that could not necessarily be reproduced by other researchers." Here Traub applied a method for developing a vaccine similar to a method that had been developed for the FMD virus.

  • "1948 - Traub starts a separate series of bacteriological experiments, much like the erysipelas, on Brucella abortus x bang bacterium, constructing a vaccine which he tested in white mice, likely with the addition of the avian influenza antigen to affect the interspecies transfer in adaptation of the bacterium to new hosts, while using the cover of vaccine research." He referred to a paper titled "Immunisierung von Mäusen gegen Brucellose mit konzentrierten Adsorbatvakzinen" ("Immunization of mice against Brucellosis with concentrated adsorbate vaccines"), but I didn't find it posted online, and I didn't even find more details about the paper in Finnegan's book. Otto Waldmann and Traub had previously developed a concentrated adsorbate vaccine for FMD at Riems Island.

  • "1950 - Traub starts his Navy work to weaponize over 40 pathogens and is place in the position of 'Supervisory Bacteriologist' for the U.S. Navy and other outfits involved in the tripartite program, to supervise simulant and live agent tests around the United States, including Plum Island, Wisconsin, Florida, Kansas, and other testing sites." In his book Finnegan cited Traub's JIOA file as evidence that Traub's title was "Supervisory Bacteriologist". The title may have been applied to microbiologists in general including virologists. In the Oxford English Dictionary, the earliest recorded use of the word "virologist" is from 1945, and the first English-language virology journal was established in 1955, so virology was still a relatively niche field in 1950.

  • "1951 (September) - Traub returns to the Northeast United States in time to supervise simulant tests on Plum Island conducting work at Plum Island releasing ticks in the biological simulant program. They were claiming to release insects infected with harmless tracer bacteria that they could track and assess how it spreads out." Then as evidence for the supposed simulant release, Finnegan presented the story from Mike Carroll's anonymous source who claimed that in 1951 Nazi scientist had released ticks on Plum Island, but it seems to be Finnegan's unique interpretation of the story that the release of ticks was some kind of a simulant experiment. Carroll only wrote that the ticks were "inoculated" but he didn't even specify if the ticks were inoculated with viruses or bacteria, even though he gave the impression the ticks were inoculated with Borrelia burgdorferi, or that the release of ticks may have been responsible for the initial seeding of the Lyme disease outbreak.

Finnegan didn't list any study where Traub modified bacteria through methods like serial passage, or where Traub would have weaponized bacteria (apart from the alleged modification of Serratia marcescens through use as a viral filter, which seems like some kind of a misinterpretation of the study).
