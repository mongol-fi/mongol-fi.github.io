Dutch article from 2003

In 2003 when Baker was living in the Netherlands and working on the Lee Harvey Oswald museum in the Netherlands, a Dutch magazine published an article about her. [https://web.archive.org/web/20051229165156/http://www.groene.nl/2003/0325/rz_kennedy.html, translated]

The article said in Dutch: "There is, however, a well-known photo of Oswald - taken in New Orleans while he was handing out pro-Castro leaflets in the city's business district - showing an unrecognizable woman. Vary Baker believes that she is the woman in that photo, but cannot prove it." She seems to have later stopped saying she was featured in the photo.

In the article she was quoted as saying "Ochsner was the first American physician to establish a link between smoking and lung cancer".

was Ochsner the first American physician to establish a link between smoking and lung cancer

The article said: "In St. Petersburg, Florida, I met two Nobel Prize winners in physics, and eventually, during an international science fair - still in high school - I was introduced to the then already legendary physician Alton Ochsner, founder of the famous Ochsner Cancer Clinic in New Orleans. [...] After meeting Ochsner, I was asked if I wanted to serve my country. From that moment on, I became an asset to the CIA, although I did not immediately realize it. Additionally, at that time, I already knew many people from the anti-Castro Cuban community."

The article quoted her as saying that David Ferrie "had lost all his hair due to illness, so he walked around with a conspicuous toupee and glued-on eyebrows". In reality he had only lost some of his hair, even though it's possible the author who interviewed Baker would've misinterpreted what she said.

I think in her 2010 book she didn't say she was asked if she wanted to serve her country after meeting Ochsner at the Science Writers Conference, but she only wrote: "The three great men told Col. Doyle that I was invited to come to Roswell Park Memorial Institute in Buffalo, New York for a summer. of fast-track training. The National Science Foundation would fund my work in Dr. Moore's own lab, where I would receive world class instruction ona project of my choice."

The article also quoted her as saying: "Lee was also involved in the research. He received a crash course on handling the transport of living cancer cells, which could be kept alive in a special chemical fluid. This technique involved working with SV-40, a material extracted from the kidneys of great apes [mensapen], which was also used in the development of the polio vaccine. The goal of the operation was to infect Castro with it. He was known as a cigar enthusiast, so no one would be surprised if he developed lung cancer." However SV40 was extracted from the kidneys of Rhesus macaques, which are an Asian species of macaques, and SV40 is not native to any great apes. It's of course possible the author would've misunderstood or mistranslated what Baker said.

One of the forms of cancer that is now associated with SV40 is mesothelioma, which is a cancer of the lining of the lungs, but SV40 was not connected to mesothelioma until the 90s. [https://www.nature.com/articles/nm0897-908] Grok said:

SV40 had only been discovered in 1960 as a contaminant in early polio vaccines. By 1963, scientists knew it could cause tumors (mainly sarcomas) when injected into newborn hamsters and other rodents. Some animal studies noted tumors in various tissues, including occasional lung involvement in hamsters, but these were undifferentiated sarcomas or other experimental tumors - not primary lung carcinoma (such as adenocarcinoma or squamous cell lung cancer).There was no established or even hypothesized link between SV40 and human lung cancer at that time.

  • The first suggestions of SV40 in human tumors (primarily mesothelioma, brain tumors, and osteosarcomas) emerged in the early 1990s.

  • Mesothelioma (a cancer of the lung lining, sometimes loosely called a "lung cancer") was not linked to SV40 until the mid-1990s.

  • Primary lung cancer itself has never been strongly or consistently associated with SV40.
