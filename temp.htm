Palts's most grandiose and covert transactions took place between 1999 and 2001. Their focal point was the Kingdom of the Netherlands.

Levicom International Holdings BV is a company registered on Leidsekade Street in central Amsterdam. In recent years, this company with a long name has sold assets located in Estonia for hundreds of millions of kroons.

These included Q-GSM mobile communications, Internet connections, cable TV, e-business, and dozens of other activities. These businesses were consolidated by the Tallinn-based company Levicom.

The chairman of Levicom's board and major owner was Tõnis Palts. He owned 31 percent of the company's shares.

The same number of shares belonged to Palts's long-time friend Toomas Peeg, who led the Res Publica election campaign during the last parliamentary elections.

The third most important shareholder was Markus Pedriks, an investment banker with roots in Saaremaa who graduated from universities in Montreal and London and received a master's degree in Switzerland.

It was on Pedriks' recommendation that Palts and Peek created Q-GSM, which Estonian bankers initially considered to be an extremely poor venture, but which later paid off handsomely.

However, the Estonian state did not receive a single cent of these enormous sums.

The reason was that Palts and his partners pulled a "little" trick. They sold Levicom's shares to the aforementioned Amsterdam company immediately before the big deal. The profit remained in the Netherlands.

Similar schemes, only with different company, city, and country names, were also used by other Estonian entrepreneurs who sold their companies to foreigners in the late 1990s. The goal was to avoid paying taxes. These men now fear the tax office more than the deadly SARS virus.

Where does the tax office stand?

In Palts's version, the Netherlands is an imposed but also very attractive option. This is because the Dutch state levies significantly lower taxes on the resale of large shareholdings than Estonia.

This is why major players such as Telia, Sonera, and NRG Energy have also come to Estonia via the Netherlands. When Eesti Energia wanted to merge with Latvenergo, moving the new company to the Netherlands was also considered.

Palts confirmed to Eesti Ekspress yesterday that he has paid all his taxes in the Netherlands. The opposite would be difficult to expect from a minister, especially the Minister of Finance.

In addition, he is protected by an agreement between Estonia and the Netherlands, according to which the same income cannot be taxed in both countries at the same time. In other words, if everything is in order in the Netherlands, there is no point in Estonian tax officials demanding millions.

However, the position of the tax authority may turn out to be quite different. If property worth hundreds of millions changes hands only for a short period of time and the profit is diverted from Estonia, this may fall under Section 84 of the Taxation Act, "Transactions and activities carried out for the purpose of tax evasion." The law states that "if the substance of a transaction or action reveals that it has been carried out for the purpose of tax evasion, the conditions of such a transaction or action that correspond to the actual economic substance of the transaction or action shall be applied for taxation purposes."
