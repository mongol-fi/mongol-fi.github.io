That's what Ethical Skeptic says when he starts his plots from 2018.

You're accidentally just calculating a crude fertility rate here and not ASFR. You're not even using any standard population. ASFR is a weighted average of age-specific fertility weights where a standard population is used as the weights.

With a real ASFR the change in slope between 2010s and 2020s is not as dramatic. But starting the x-axis from 2010 is still misleading, because the 2010-2019 linear trend has a poor fit to the 2000-2019 curved trend.

What's up with the ultra-broad 30-48 age group? Broad age groups are not needed with full national data where there's no age with a small population. In the ONS dataset they're needed because there's some combinations of vaccination status, age, and month with a small population.

What he should've done is to calculate a weighted average of the age-specific fertility rates where a standard population is used as the weights. Here I used the 2020 population estimates as the standard population:

born=fread("births_by_years_and_mothers_age_groups.csv")
pop=fread("women_population_by_age_groups_R.csv")
me=merge(born,pop)

# what canceledmouse did
p1=me[,.(wrong_asfr=sum(births)/sum(total_population)*1000),year]

# what he should've done
std=me[year==2020,total_population/sum(total_population)]
me[,std:=std[factor(age_group)]]
p2=me[,.(asfr=sum(births/total_population*std*1000)),year]

print(round(merge(p1,p2),1),r=F)
