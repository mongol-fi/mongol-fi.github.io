Finnegan's response to my arguments about anserina evolving into burgdorferi

Finnegan posted response to the section of my website were I wrote that Traub cannot have created Borrelia burgdorferi from Borrelia anserina. [https://awfinnegan.substack.com/p/clarification-on-the-lyme-borreliosis]

He wrote:

Furthermore, there are elements of this story that no one has ever studied - mainly in the highly sophisticated conditions created by Erich Traub and his German colleagues during WWII, when he began adapting multiple overlapping infections from different sources into the same tick vector like a Russian-doll cocktail, with the addition of bacteriophage varieties (including lyophilized or dry-frozen bacteriophages),[6,7] viruses,[8] and mycoplasma,9 all of which Insel Riems was working with during Traub's tenure as Vice President - overseeing the entire phase of Insel Riems research in the WWII years and well into the beginnings of the Cold War under Soviet occupation. The addition of viruses, phages, and mycoplasma would act as gene shuttles between the different co-infecting agents, allowing for rapid evolution and gene swapping between numerous foreign pathogens that had not been exposed to each other previously. Such controlled environments could greatly accelerate mutation rates, adapt pathogens to many new hosts, change its tropisms, and take on a myriad of new characteristics - making short-term changes appear to be long-term evolutionary distance.

To add another layer of complexity, once these extremely unique and unusual biological specimens were introduced into wild habitats of ecosystems in vastly different parts of the globe by nations duplicating or conducting further biowarfare research and testing activities, it further complicates the theory of natural evolution with yet even more highly divergent and unique conditions from the native viruses and microbes in each specific ecosystem where these vectors are released. This could diversify further what seem like millions of years of evolution when in fact were just a few years of highly exceptional conditions duplicated across varying climatic conditions within a few years in their respective biological warfare programs.

I showed the text above to Grok and asked: "If Borrelia burgdorferi was created by bioweaponeers less than 100 years ago from Borrelia anserina, could conditions like this have caused burgdorferi to have only around 75% nucleotide similarity across the whole chromosome from anserina? Paragraphs-only answer." I got this answer:

No, the conditions described - co-infecting ticks with layered pathogens, bacteriophages, viruses, and mycoplasma as potential gene shuttles under controlled WWII-era bioweapon research - could not have produced a Borrelia burgdorferi chromosome showing only around 75% nucleotide similarity to that of Borrelia anserina in less than a century. Bacterial whole-chromosome average nucleotide identity (ANI) at this level reflects deep evolutionary divergence between the Lyme-associated Borreliella group and the relapsing-fever Borrelia group, a separation that phylogenomic data place millions of years in the past through gradual accumulation of mutations, insertions, deletions, and rearrangements under natural selective pressures. Even aggressive horizontal gene transfer mediated by phages or other mobile elements transfers relatively small genomic segments at a time and cannot wholesale rewrite hundreds of kilobases across an entire linear chromosome while preserving the precise synteny, telomere structure, replication machinery, and host-vector adaptation required for a viable spirochete.

Rapid "gene swapping" in a Russian-doll tick cocktail might accelerate acquisition of a few accessory genes or plasmids from co-infecting microbes, but it cannot generate the genome-wide sequence divergence observed. Achieving 25% nucleotide dissimilarity from a near-identical starting anserina genome would demand either hypermutator-level error rates sustained across thousands of generations without lethal collapse or massive, coordinated replacement of core chromosomal regions with entirely unrelated sequences - neither of which is biologically feasible in laboratory or field release settings spanning mere decades. Natural mutation rates, even elevated under stress or mutagenesis, fall orders of magnitude short of the required changes; phage-mediated transduction and mycoplasma- or virus-assisted shuttling simply lack the capacity to orchestrate such precise, large-scale remodeling without destroying the organism's functionality.

The sophisticated overlapping-infection protocols pioneered by researchers like Erich Traub undoubtedly enabled adaptation to new hosts and vectors, potentially speeding niche shifts or antigenic variation, yet these processes still operate within the constraints of existing genetic mechanisms. They could not compress millions of years of apparent evolutionary distance into a few years of exceptional conditions, nor could subsequent global releases into varied ecosystems retroactively manufacture the consistent phylogenetic distance now measured between the two species. In short, the 75% chromosomal similarity is a signature of ancient natural divergence, not a hallmark of recent bioweapon engineering.

Finnegan also wrote: "As is common in top secret research and development, advances in science were often known many decades before their public disclosures. I believe Insel Riems and the Germans were already up to speed on current modern-day bioengineering back in the days of World War II, as Insel Riems was working with several bacteriophage varieties and Mycoplasma. Both would act to shuttle genes between the different cocktail of pathogens and accelerate rapid evolution." However there is no method even in modern-day bioengineering to accelerate evolution by 6 or 7 orders of magnitude, so you can create a new species of bacteria that is 25% divergent from another species across the whole chromosome within a time span of a few years.

Finnegan wrote:

Erich Traub had also known that he could use viruses to adapt microorganisms to new species of animals through the formation of heterokaryons. He likely taught this technique to Iranian scientist G. K. Kanhai while in Iran in the late 1960s - teaching him how to change the properties of FMD virus to take on a whole array of new properties, adapt other pathogens to new species and beyond. As Traub said in his collaborative work with Kanhai, just some of the factors he was able to change with ease were:

  1. natural retrogression and termination of epizootics;
  2. increase in potency of the causative agent;
  3. virus adaptation to certain species of animals and escalation or decrease of infectivity and pathogenicity for other species;
  4. different affinity of virus strains for certain cells (cardiac FMD);
  5. differences in susceptibility among individual animals of the same species;
  6. reduced susceptibility in undernourished individuals;
  7. different behavior of different virus types;
  8. persistent infection;
  9. serological variation.[10]

What the paper he linked actually said was that "in the course of this work with cells, observations were made which may have a bearing on certain epidemiological events in the field", which included phenomena like "termination of epizootics" (i.e. ending of an animal disease outbreak): [https://sci-hub.ee/https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/j.1439-0450.1968.tb00327.x]

Traub didn't claim that he had developed a method to end outbreaks of diseases in animals. But he pointed out that serial passage led to a gradual attenuation of the pathogen so eventually it was not able to sustain another passage, which he compared to the natural weakening of a pathogen during a disease outbreak in animals.

Finnegan continued:

Some years later, Kanhai then went on to publish about the use of Sendai virus to adapt the tick-borne disease Theileria parva to a new host via cell-fusion, or the creation of heterokaryons.[10]

The study did not result in T. parva being adapted to hamsters. Grok said:

What it actually demonstrated was a short-term, artificial laboratory trick: using UV-inactivated Sendai virus to force the fusion of T. parva-infected bovine lymphoid cells with uninfected baby hamster kidney (BHK) cells (or mouse cells in related work). This created transient heterokaryons - hybrid cells with mixed bovine and hamster nuclei sharing a common cytoplasm. In these fused cells, the intracellular parasites could be detected for a limited time, sometimes appearing in regions that looked like they contained mostly hamster nuclei.

This was a proof-of-concept for moving a strictly cell-associated parasite across a species barrier in vitro under highly artificial conditions. It did not result in:

  • Stable, continuous propagation of T. parva in hamster (or mouse) cell lines without the bovine component.

  • Genetic adaptation or evolutionary change in the parasite itself that allowed it to infect or replicate independently in a new host species.

  • Successful infection of live hamsters or any whole-animal model with a viable, transmissible T. parva strain.

Subsequent papers by Irvin, Brown, Kanhai, Stagg and colleagues (e.g., 1975-1976 attempts with mouse Ehrlich ascites tumour cells or further hamster fusions) explicitly explored whether they could generate truly infected, replicating rodent cell lines. These efforts largely failed to produce stable, productive infections; the heterokaryons were short-lived, and the parasites did not establish independent growth in the new cellular environment.

Theileria parva remains highly host-specific to bovines (cattle and African buffalo) under natural conditions. Its schizont stage transforms and replicates inside bovine lymphocytes, and no routine, stable rodent or hamster culture system for T. parva ever emerged from this line of work. The technique was a clever virological tool for studying intracellular parasite behavior in controlled settings, not a method for creating a new, adapted pathogen capable of sustained infection in a different species.
