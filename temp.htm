Finnegan wrote: "Borrelia anserina is morphologically quite different from all the other Relapsing Fever Borrelia in that the other Relapsing Fever spirochetes are notably thicker and contain far more flagella that are not sheathed and have sharply pointed ends."

  • "Borrelia anserina is morphologically quite different from all the other Relapsing Fever Borrelia" - Not generally true. B. anserina is considered a member of the relapsing fever lineage, and while it has some biological peculiarities (avian host, Argas tick vector), it is not recognized as morphologically distinct from all other relapsing fever borreliae.
  • "the other Relapsing Fever spirochetes are notably thicker" - Overstated. There are reported differences in average dimensions among Borrelia species, but there is substantial overlap. Some relapsing fever species may be somewhat larger than B. burgdorferi on average, but "notably thicker" is not a consistent or diagnostic feature across the group.
  • "contain far more flagella" - Not established. Different Borrelia species vary somewhat in the number of periplasmic flagella, but there is no accepted generalization that all relapsing fever borreliae have "far more" flagella than B. anserina or Lyme borreliae.
  • "that are not sheathed" - False. All Borrelia species have periplasmic (endoflagellar) flagella located within the periplasmic space, beneath the outer membrane. They are not exposed externally like the flagella of many other bacteria. The flagellar filaments themselves are surrounded by the periplasm and outer membrane in both Lyme and relapsing fever borreliae.
  • "have sharply pointed ends" - Not a recognized distinguishing feature. The cell ends of some Borrelia species can appear more tapered than others, but this is variable and not a standard morphological criterion separating relapsing fever borreliae from B. anserina or Lyme borreliae.

ChatGPT said:

No. There is no evidence that the Lyme borreliosis (LB) group is orders of magnitude more abundant than the relapsing fever (RF) group in terms of the total number of bacterial cells existing in nature. That quantity has never been estimated, and there are insufficient ecological data to make such a comparison.

Although Lyme borreliae likely infect a much larger number of vertebrate hosts and ticks because of their broad distribution across the Northern Hemisphere, relapsing fever borreliae often reach far higher concentrations within infected hosts. During acute relapsing fever, spirochete densities in the bloodstream can be millions to hundreds of millions of cells per milliliter of blood, whereas Borrelia burgdorferi typically occurs at much lower levels in mammalian blood and resides primarily in tissues.

As a result, a greater number of infected hosts does not necessarily translate into a greater total number of bacterial cells. Without comprehensive data on the number of infected hosts and vectors, infection prevalence, bacterial burdens, and infection durations for all Borrelia species, there is no scientific basis for claiming that the Lyme borreliosis group is orders of magnitude more abundant than the relapsing fever group in nature.

Henjin's simple laboratory adaptation experiments are terrible comparisons to the dynamic situation I am presenting. When the entire argument that "Borrelia burgdorferi could not have come from Borrelia anserina" is based solely on the genome distance alone and an assumed slow-clock model of evolution under an assumed fixed mutation rate of small-scale point mutations as though they stay constant and gradually form new species slowly, it has serious limitations and almost sounds absurd because it totally fails to consider just how dynamic and unpredictable the life of a microbe is under different forms of stress, selection pressure, host and vector switching, and transitions of all kinds. There are instances in bacteria like that seen in the Lyme clade of Borrelia where it can function under completely different rules under selection pressure, rather than by fixed and steady small-scale mutations, they have backup strategies in certain circumstances that allow them to quickly evolve based on a dynamic set of factors.

Why wasn't Borrelia burgdorferi discovered until 1982?

Finnegan wrote: "All the way up to the 1950s the black-legged tick was not a known transmitter of any known human diseases,[33] even though according to the slow evolution theory of it evolving over millions of years the Lyme disease spirochete should have been by this time at least found, and more so, found to be increasing, since it is now the most abundant Borrelia spirochete found throughout the globe and yet not a single sample was declared until 1982.[34]"

When I asked why Borrelia burgdorferi wasn't discovered until 1982 even though Borrelia anserina had been discovered much earlier, Grok produced this list of possible explanations:

  • Lyme disease itself was not recognized as a distinct clinical entity until the mid-1970s. A cluster of unusual juvenile arthritis cases in and around Lyme, Connecticut (starting around 1975), prompted investigation by researchers like Allen Steere and Stephen Malawista at Yale. They linked it to tick bites and a characteristic "bull's-eye" rash (erythema migrans). Prior to this, the disease wasn't on the radar as a specific syndrome in the U.S., even though similar skin and neurological conditions (e.g., erythema migrans, acrodermatitis chronica atrophicans) had been described in Europe since the late 1800s/early 1900s. [https://medicine.yale.edu/news-article/bullseye-how-yale-researchers-identified-lyme-disease/]

  • The pathogen is fastidious and hard to culture/isolate. B. burgdorferi is a spirochete that is difficult to grow in the lab. It requires specific conditions and media (like the Barbour-Stoenner-Kelly or BSK medium, which Barbour helped refine). Earlier attempts to find a causative agent (suspecting viruses, other bacteria, or even rickettsia) failed because the organism wasn't easily visible or culturable from patient samples without targeted efforts. In contrast, B. anserina was more readily observable and culturable in bird hosts or eggs, as it causes overt disease in poultry and was studied in veterinary/agricultural contexts. [https://www.bostonglobe.com/ideas/2019/08/22/lyme-disease-how-discovered-and-where-from-here/KVovTbvJsJ8HIhx4K7yYQI/story.html]

  • Vector and ecology specifics. Lyme borreliosis is transmitted by Ixodes "hard" ticks (e.g., I. scapularis in the eastern U.S.), which have a complex multi-year life cycle involving small mammal reservoirs (like white-footed mice). The connection to these specific ticks and the disease's epidemiology only solidified in the 1970s. B. anserina is transmitted by Argas soft ticks and was easier to link to outbreaks in domestic birds. Burgdorfer's team found the spirochetes while dissecting Ixodes ticks from endemic areas (initially investigating other tick-borne diseases like Rocky Mountain spotted fever or babesiosis). [https://irp.nih.gov/accomplishments/discovery-of-the-disease-agent-causing-lyme-disease]

  • Technological and research focus limitations. Before the 1970s outbreak investigation, there was no strong incentive or serological/epidemiological framework to hunt for this specific organism in humans or ticks. Earlier European observations of spirochetes in erythema migrans lesions (e.g., 1940s-1950s) and successful penicillin treatment suggested a bacterial cause, but definitive isolation came later. The 1981-1982 work succeeded due to combined expertise in tick dissection (Burgdorfer), spirochete cultivation (Barbour), and access to patient sera/ticks from endemic areas (e.g., via Jorge Benach). [https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7946767/]

  • Historical context of Borrelia research. Other Borrelia species (relapsing fever agents and B. anserina) were discovered in the 19th/early 20th centuries because they caused more dramatic, epidemic-like diseases in humans or livestock, often with visible spirochetemia (high numbers in blood). Lyme disease presents with subtler, slower-progressing symptoms (rash, arthritis, neurological issues) that can mimic other conditions, delaying recognition. [https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC13209959/]
