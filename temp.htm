Erich Traub's involvement in research of tick-borne diseases

When I asked Grok what evidence Finnegan's Traub timeline contained that Traub was involved in research into Borrelia or tick-borne diseases, Grok said: [https://www.thesleeperagent.com/erich-traub]

The document (The Sleeper Agent timeline) presents the following claims as evidence. None describe Traub personally conducting extensive hands-on experiments weaponizing or modifying tick-borne agents; most are indirect, supervisory, or interpretive.

1. 1939 - Learning about tick-borne diseases from Karl Beller

  • Claim: Karl Beller taught Traub about tick-borne diseases including Rickettsia, Babesia, Bartonella, Ehrlichia, and different ticks for spreading them. The chapter "Heartwater and Other Animal Rickettsioses" (in the Handbook of Virus Diseases) is cited. It allegedly showed how multiple infections can be combined into one complex disease and that hard-bodied ticks can spread spirochetes.
  • Interpretation in document: These agents (including Babesia) were later used in "Traub's weaponized ticks."

2. 1939 - Access to avian spirochete (Borrelia anserina)

  • Claim: Werner Schäfer (working under Traub and Beller at Giessen) experimented with avian spirochetes Spirochaeta gallinarum (Borrelia anserina). Paper cited: Schäfer 1939 - The Maintenance of Spirochaeta gallinarum by Passages in Fowl Embryos. Schäfer received the strain from Dr. Franz Jahnel; Traub and Beller were his superiors.
  • Interpretation in document: "It is here where we find Traub in possession of the avian spirochetes that were later weaponized to become Borrelia burgdorferi."

3. 1941 - Mention of blood parasites in the horse respiratory catarrh paper

  • Claim: During an outbreak in Army horses, Traub and colleagues set up "experiments on blood parasites", equine infectious anemia, and infectious respiratory catarrh. Paper: Infectious Respiratory Catarrh in the Horses of a Veterinary Company (Traub 1941).
  • Interpretation in document: Links this to direct experience with agents like Babesia (a common equine blood parasite) and mixed infections that could confuse diagnosis.

4. Contextual / indirect references (no specific Traub paper on ticks or Borrelia)

  • 1953 USDA poultry conference lists avian spirochetosis (Borrelia anserina) as a "special agent" of biological warfare concern. The document claims this proves Traub's earlier avian Borrelia work was viewed as a bioweapon threat and that Lyme disease (B. burgdorferi) originated from it.
  • Vague references to Traub's 1950s U.S. Navy work supervising "over 40 pathogens and rickettsia," interpreted as including tick-borne diseases.
  • Later papers on immune tolerance, LCM virus, EEE, etc., are sometimes tied narratively to "stealth" mixed/co-infections that could involve tick-borne pathogens, but no direct research on ticks/Borrelia is described.

Key Limitations Noted

  • All links are indirect (supervisory role, learning from others, incidental differential diagnosis).
  • No primary quotes or descriptions show Traub isolating, passaging, genetically altering, or weaponizing Borrelia, Babesia, or any other tick-borne pathogen.
  • The 1941 horse paper mentions blood parasites only in passing as part of ruling out differentials in a respiratory outbreak - it is not a dedicated study on Babesia or spirochetes.

So basically none of the four points showed that Traub himself was involved in research on Borrelia:

There was a chapter, 'Heartwater and other animal rickettsioses.' It is here that Erich Traub gained his important research on ticks and tick-borne disease." But Finnegan didn't cite any source, and he didn't present evidence that Traub even read the chapter about rickettsia in the book, or that Beller did research on rickettsia during the time he worked with Traub. The book has an 18-page chapter titled "Herzwasser und sonstige tierische Rickettsiosen. Von Prof. Dr. K. BELLER - Gießen". He teaches Traub how a complex disease results when many infections are rolled into one, citing a case where a dog was simultaneously infected with Rickettsia, Babesia, Bartonella, Ehrlichia, and Leishmaniasis, while also showing that it is possible to get hard-bodied ticks to spread spirochetes.   3. In his book Finnegan wrote: "Traub mentions extensive experiments on horses with blood parasites in a 1941 paper, linking him to direct experience with disease agents like Babesia.[16] Adding a weaponized avian relapsing fever spirochete (Borrelia anserina) to the mix would cause a complex mix of infections that would not only be very confusing to physicians and diagnosis, but also very resistant to treatment." On his website he wrote: "An outbreak of unknown origin occurs in Army horses owned by a veterinary company, with horses showing signs of broncho-pneumonia. They set up a handful of experiments to assess the cause of disease. They set up experiments on blood parasites, equine infectious anemia, and infectious respiratory catarrh (mixed infections). They conclude the disease was due to infectious respiratory catarrh, after finding Streptococcus equi, Streptococcus pyogenes, and other bacterium that are usually considered 'harmless' to the horse." [https://www.thesleeperagent.com/erich-traub] The paper he cited was titled in German "Infectious Respiratory Catarrh in the Horses of a Veterinary Company". Infectious respiratory catarrh in horses is typically caused by viral or bacterial agents, like in the study Finnegan wrote about. But because the study also investigated blood parasites as an alternative etiology, and Babesia is caused by tick-borne blood parasites, Finnegan said the study linked Finnegan "to direct experience with disease agents like Babesia" (but not even Babesia itself).

Traub wrote that a JIOA file on Traub's assistant Anne-Lise Bürger said: "Duties of the position to be filled by Miss Burger are concerned with the supervision of maintenance of approximately 40 strains of virus and rickettsias [sic] in serial egg and animal passages in order to provide stocks of infectious materials for experimental work in [the] virology division and other divisions of the Naval Medical Research Institute." However it's not clear if Bürger would've even maintained strains of rickettsia for Traub or for other researchers. The file mentions "other divisions of the Naval Medical Research Institute", even though Traub worked for the virology division.

On his timeline Finnegan wrote that following a conference in 1939, "there is a total blackout of research on Borrelia anserina by Plum Island and the USDA. The lack of any research on Borrelia anserina and Lyme disease (Borrelia burgdorferi) by Plum Island is itself highly suspect, because Plum Island was set up to investigate foreign animal diseases, especially those with zoonotic potential. That means Plum Island should have been heavily involved in its research but there is not so much of a mention of it in any Plum Island work from that point forward.
